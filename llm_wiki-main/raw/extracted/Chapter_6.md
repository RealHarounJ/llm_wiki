) Forecasting a Project’s Cash Flows NPVs can be used to determine whether a business project is worth undertaking. In this context, the concept of NPV is crucial in capital budgeting decisions. Capital budgeting refers to the process of investing money in fixed assets, such as infrastructure and production investments like plants, machinery, equipment, and so on—essentially, all those investments that will be recovered over the long term and form part of the structure of a firm or company. For capital budgeting, you need to forecast future cash flows. In a business project, as with any other kind of investment, you invest money today with the expectation of recovering that money in the future through a stream of cash flows. These future cash flows arise from the investment’s ability to allow the company to produce goods, and the sale of these goods generates revenue, which then helps recover the initial investment. We have seen that an investment in any asset creates wealth if the discounted value of the future cash flows exceeds the up-front cost. Up to this point, however, we have glossed over the problem of what to discount. When you are faced with this problem, you should stick to five general rules: Rule 1: Discount Cash Flows, Not Profits Net present value depends on the expected future cash flow. Cash flow is simply the difference between cash received and cash paid out. So, only cash flow is relevant in business and capital budgeting decisions. Why? The point is, when assessing an investment project, we need to focus on cash, not profit, for two main reasons: Profit is not money. You can’t buy things with profit; you buy things with cash. Profit can be influenced by subjective, discretionary decisions. For example, when calculating depreciation, amortisation, or valuing inventories, the profit figure can be influenced by the perspective or accounting decisions of those responsible for preparing the financial statements. This makes profit less reliable for decision-making purposes. For these reasons, only cash flow is relevant when assessing a business project. Moreover, the cash flow from a business project must be calculated on an incremental (or differential) basis. In other words, you need to ask, What happens if I undertake this project? What is the change in cash flow if the project goes ahead? We are only concerned with the incremental value created by the project, both in terms of cash inflows and cash outflows. To asses the impact of a project, it is necessary to forecast the new inflows and outflows that are directly associated with that project. Capital Expenditures Accountants separate expenses into current expenditures (e.g., wages) and capital expenditures (e.g., machinery). Current expenses are deducted from revenues immediately, but capital expenses are not. Instead of treating a large asset purchase as a loss, accountants recognise that the company is simply exchanging cash for an asset of equal value. To reflect the gradual use of the asset, its cost is spread over its useful life through depreciation. For example, a million machine may be depreciated at per year for 10 years, even though the full cash payment occurred upfront. When calculating NPV, state capital expenditures when they occur, not later when they show up as depreciation. Capital expenditure affects cash flow but not profits, while depreciation affects profits but not cash flow. To go from accounting profit to cash flow, you need to add back depreciation (which is not a cash outflow) and subtract capital expenditure (which is a cash outflow). Working Capital Net working capital is the difference between a company’s short-term assets and its short-term liabilities. Most projects entail an investment in net working capital. This investment is a cash outflow, just like an investment in capital equipment. Therefore, each period’s change in working capital should be recognised in your cash-flow forecasts. By the same token, when the project comes to an end, you can usually recover some of the investment. Rule 2: Discount Incremental Cash Flows and Ignore Non-Incremental Cash Flows Do not confuse average with incremental payoffs. Do not fall in love with previous projects. Never rely on past data. Financial managers must update all the information to make sure that new project is still worth undertaking otherwise, you wouldn't do your job as you should. To assess this, we need to understand whether, for example, we need to replace an entire machine or buy new equipment for a new project. In such cases, we must evaluate if the whole project, all the investments, and all the profits are still valuable—that is, if they still generate positive NPVs. In other words, the focus should be on the incremental value, not the average, to determine whether the investment remains worthwhile. Sometimes new projects imply after-sale cash flows Such as selling spare parts or offering maintenance services. For example, after selling a product like a plant or machinery, the company might earn cash inflows from selling parts or offering maintenance over time. It's important to consider these potential future inflows when evaluating a new project. Also, opportunity costs must be included in the analysis In this case, opportunity cost refers to what the company forgoes by choosing to undertake a new project. Suppose the company owns a large piece of land that could be sold to a farmer or another buyer. Instead of selling the land, the company decides to build a new facility on it. The opportunity cost here is the money the company loses from not selling the land. The company gives up the sale price, which would have been an inflow, because it chooses to use the land for a new project. This lost sale is considered an opportunity cost. Sometimes opportunity costs may be very difficult to estimate. However, where the resource can be freely traded, its opportunity cost is simply equal to the market price. Sunk costs are costs that have already been incurred in the past and cannot be recovered. Since they can’t be changed by any future decision, they should not be considered when calculating the NPV (Net Present Value) of a project. When evaluating a new project, focus only on the future costs and revenues that will be affected by the decision. Allocated overhead costs refer to general costs (like administrative or utility expenses) that support many different projects. Sometimes, when a company has multiple ongoing or potential projects, these overhead costs need to be distributed or allocated across them. The challenge is deciding how much of the general overhead costs should be attributed to each individual project For example, if a company has several projects underway and shares common resources like office space, electricity, or administrative support, the company needs to decide how to divide those shared costs across the various projects. These calculations are important to ensure that each project accurately reflects its share of the general costs, which in turn influences decisions about whether the project is worth undertaking Salvage value refers to the residual value of an asset, such as machinery, at the end of its useful life. It represents how much you can sell the equipment for on the second-hand market after completing the project. In some cases, the salvage value can even be negative. This can occur if, when the project is finished, you must pay to dispose of or decommission the asset. For example, if the project involves machinery or equipment that must be disposed of in a specific way, such as environmentally harmful materials or activities, you may need to pay to have it dismantled or properly disposed of. This could involve hiring specialists to manage the disposal process legally and safely. In this case, the cost of disposal could exceed the resale value, resulting in a negative salvage value. This includes determining how much money will be required for the investment, for example, in buying new machinery, hiring staff, or other capital expenses. These investments will generate future cash inflows. Financing decisions: After planning the investment, you need to determine how to raise the required funds. You might need to obtain capital by taking out a loan from a bank, issuing new shares to shareholders, or a combination of both. This is where financing decisions come in. You must evaluate the best way to secure the funds, considering the costs of debt versus equity, as well as the risk and return for the company. If you fund a project using some debt, you shouldn’t count the borrowed money as part of the project's cash inflows, nor should you deduct interest and principal repayments as cash outflows. Instead, you should evaluate the project as if it were entirely funded by equity. This means considering only the project's actual cash flows—what it earns and what it costs— without factoring in how it’s financed. This approach keeps the focus on the project's true financial performance, separate from any specific financing decision Rule 5: Forecast Cash Flows after Taxes Taxes are an expense just like wages and raw materials. Therefore, cash flows should be estimated on an after-tax basis. You need to subtract tax payments from pre-tax cash flows and discount the net amount. How to Choose between Competing Projects Here, the work of the financial manager is not finished yet, because even if you have forecasted a positive NPV project, you still need to make another decisions. Problem 1: The Investment Timing Decision The fact that a project has a positive NPV does not mean that it is best undertaken now. It might be even more valuable if undertaken in the future. By investing now, you can begin to realise the returns sooner, starting the project immediately and capturing potential early benefits. Waiting allows some of your forecasts to become actual events, which can make your predictions more accurate. As a result, you have the opportunity to make more precise predictions and reduce the uncertainty in your project’s future cash flows. This can increase the probability of generating value, as the NPV is based on forecasts and predictions, and no one can predict the future with certainty.
