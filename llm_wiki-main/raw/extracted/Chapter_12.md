The Efficient Market Hypothesis An efficient market is one in which share prices fully reflect all available information about the company issuing the shares. This information can include both positive and negative news, and it is quickly incorporated into the share price as soon as it becomes known. Competition between traders will tend to produce an efficient market. In such a market, prices will rapidly impound any new information, and it will be difficult to make consistently superior returns. Forms of Market Efficiency We’ve already emphasised one important component of the market efficiency definition: An efficient market incorporates all available information. But what about the second component, “available”? What do we mean by available information? There are three ways to define it, which in turn implies three forms of market efficiency. Weak-form efficiency The weak form of market efficiency means that current share prices reflect all past prices and returns. So, today’s price already incorporates all historical price information. This implies that you cannot use past prices to predict future prices and make abnormal profits because any information you could have inferred from past prices is already in the current stock price. So, knowing past stock prices doesn’t help you predict future returns. Suppose you believe a share is currently undervalued because it doesn’t reflect previous price movements. You might expect the price to rise soon and try to invest to earn a profit. But in a weak-form efficient market, this cannot happen, because the current price already reflects all past movements. There are no patterns or predictable corrections you can exploit—the market has already priced them in. Semi-strong-form efficiency The semi-strong form of market efficiency means that share prices reflect all publicly available information. This includes not only past prices and movements, as in the weak form, but also all other public information, such as Statements from the chief executive officer (CEO), Public announcements or earnings reports, News and information from the mass media, Analyst reports, and more. In this form, the market adjusts quickly to new public information, and investors cannot gain an advantage by trading on information that is publicly available. Any new public information is already reflected in the share price almost immediately. Strong-form efficiency This implies that no investor, not even company insiders such as the chief executive officer (CEO), can consistently earn excess returns because all information—regardless of how it is obtained—is already incorporated into the share price. As a result, no one has a true informational advantage, and the best strategy for investors in such a market is to diversify. Diversification reduces risk and allows investors to earn the market average return, which is all that can be expected in a perfectly efficient market. In contrast, if markets were not strongly efficient, insiders with access to private information—such as CEOs—could exploit it to buy undervalued shares and earn excess profits, which violates the (1 + r) t Corporate Finance 96 principle of strong-form efficiency. Whether a piece of information counts as public or private is sometimes a grey area, so the line between strong-form and semi-strong-form efficiency is blurred. What is the evidence on market efficiency? On one side, some professionals argue that the strong form of market efficiency often holds. They point to empirical studies showing that, on average, institutional investors such as mutual funds, banks, and insurance companies do not consistently achieve returns higher than the overall market. This suggests that even the most informed and educated investors cannot outperform the market in the long run, which supports the idea that prices already reflect all available information. On the other hand, some critics believe that markets tend to be inefficient. Why? Information asymmetry: Even if retail investors put effort into gathering and analysing market data, they cannot access the same level of information as insiders—such as members of the board of directors . Therefore, information is not equally distributed among all market participants. Differences in analytical ability: Not all investors have the same skills or tools to process and interpret information correctly. As a result, mistakes are made, leading to mispricing. Behavioural factors and herd behaviour: At times, investors do not act rationally. Instead, they follow the crowd, leading to herd effects. This can drive prices away from their fundamental values, causing bubbles or crashes. Another important point is that, from a psychological perspective, people tend to be more concerned about losses than motivated by potential gains. In other words, investors are often satisfied with a small profit and prefer to sell shares earlier than they should, simply to avoid the risk of losing. As a result, they may sell too soon, preventing share prices from fully rising to reflect all the available information. This behaviour contributes to market inefficiency, as it causes prices to fall prematurely, rather than adjusting naturally to the true value implied by the information. behavioural finance Behavioural finance is a relatively new field in corporate finance, and it stands in contrast to the efficient market hypothesis. Researchers have attempted to verify the presence of the three forms of market efficiency—weak, semistrong, and strong—in order to support their respective positions. To test weak-form market efficiency, researchers used econometric models to determine whether past share prices were related to current prices. Their findings revealed no such relationship or correlation. In other words, share prices follow a random walk therefore past prices cannot predict future prices. To examine semi-strong form market efficiency, researchers assessed the speed at which share prices adjust to new public information—whether positive or negative. They observed that price changes tend to occur very quickly, indicating that markets react almost immediately to new information. This provided evidence in support of the semi-strong form of efficiency. As for the strong-form market efficiency, researchers found that, on average, even highly informed and professional investors—such as mutual funds, insurance companies, and banks—do not consistently outperform the market in the long run. This suggests that no single investor has privileged access to information, and thus supports the notion that prices reflect all available information, both public and private. Implications of Market Efficiency Stock Prices Follow Random Walks Corporate Finance 97 If markets are weak-form efficient, then stock prices are random walks—price changes are random and, in particular, are unrelated to past price changes. As investors try to take advantage of any information in past prices, prices adjust immediately until the superior profits from studying price movements disappear. As a result, all the information in past prices will be reflected in today’s stock price, not tomorrow’s. Patterns in prices will no longer exist, and price changes in one period will be independent of changes in the next. In other words, the share price will follow a random walk. Firm Financing Decisions Neither Create Nor Destroy Value Financial managers are sometimes tempted to base financing decisions on perceived market conditions rather than on sound investment logic. Corporate Finance 98 For instance, in a bullish market, a manager may be tempted to issue new shares to take advantage of higher share prices and raise more capital. Conversely, in a bearish market, the same manager might prefer to borrow from a bank or issue bonds instead of issuing shares. However, this reasoning is flawed. Financing decisions should not be driven by short-term market trends—bullish or bearish conditions. Rather, a financial manager should first assess whether there are valuable investment projects available. Only after confirming this, should they decide how to raise the necessary capital—either through debt or equity. In other words, capital should only be raised when there are projects that justify the investment, not in response to temporary market conditions. The value of an investment decision is given purely by the NPV of that investment; the NPV of the accompanying financial decision is zero. An overview of corporate financing The two primary sources of corporate finance are: Debt – Borrowed funds that must be repaid with interest. Debt has a significant impact on the cash flows generated by a firm, as interest payments and capital repayments take priority over dividends and equity repayments. Interest and capital repayments are scheduled, meaning they must be made when due and cannot be postponed. In contrast, equity is typically repaid only at the end of a firm’s life, and dividends are distributed only if shareholders decide to do so. Normally, debt does not come with control rights. Corporate Finance 99 Debtholders have significant claims on the firm’s cash flows, but they do not have voting power or control over corporate decisions. However, in some cases, banks or other lenders may impose restrictions on a firm's financial policy. For example, a bank that provides a large amount of debt capital may set covenants that limit the firm’s ability to take on additional debt or distribute dividends. There are many forms of debt. For example, we can distinguish between short-term and long-term debt. Other distinctions include fixed and floating debt. Floating debt means that interest payments are based on a variable benchmark rate or index. Therefore, if the benchmark rate increases, interest payments also increase; if it decreases, interest payments decrease accordingly. Debt can also be issued in a foreign currency. For instance, a European company may borrow in US dollars or Japanese yen. Another important distinction relates to the priority of repayment among different debt holders. This is referred to as seniority. Senior debt is repaid before other types of debt, such as junior or subordinated debt. In cases of financial distress or liquidation, senior debt holders have a higher claim on the firm’s assets and are repaid before junior debt holders. Equity – Capital raised by issuing shares of the company. internal sources of finance: Internal sources arise when a firm reinvests its profits rather than distributing them as dividends. If a firm is able to generate revenues that exceed its costs, it will have a positive profit. Generating a positive difference between revenues and costs. Control Rights: While debt holders do not have voting rights, they may influence the firm indirectly. Financial markets and intermediaries Financial Markets A financial market is a market where securities—such as bonds, stocks, and other financial instruments —are issued, offered, traded, bought, and sold. In financial markets, we distinguish between primary issues Primary issues refer to the issuance of new securities, such as new shares, through which enterprises raise new capital from investors. This is the process by which firms collect funds directly from the market. secondary transactions. On the other hand, secondary transactions involve the trading of existing securities. In this case, no new capital is raised by the firm. Instead, the ownership of the securities changes hands between investors—one investor sells, and another buys. There is an important relationship between primary issues and secondary transactions. The efficiency of the secondary market enhances the attractiveness and effectiveness of the primary market. If you are a potential investor in newly issued securities, you are more likely to invest if you know that you can later sell those securities quickly, at a fair price, and with ease. In other words, a well-functioning and liquid secondary market increases investor confidence, which in turn facilitates the success of new issues in the primary market. Financial intermediaries Financial intermediaries are institutions such as mutual funds, pension funds, banks, and insurance companies. Their main function is to collect money from investors—primarily households and individuals—and then allocate that capital to those who need it, such as firms, other households, or even banks. Let’s begin with one specific category: mutual funds. Mutual funds are investment vehicles managed by financial institutions. These institutions collect money from investors and pool it into a common fund, which is then invested in a diversified portfolio of assets. Corporate Finance 101 Investors who contribute to the mutual fund receive a proportional (global) share of the overall assets held by the fund A key feature of mutual funds is that they are typically open-ended investments. This means that investors can redeem their shares in the fund at any time, either by selling them on the market or by requesting payment directly from the fund management company. Similarly, investors can increase their stake in the fund by contributing more capital. There is a wide variety of mutual funds, tailored to different investment strategies and preferences. For example, some funds invest exclusively in European, Asian, or U.S. securities. Others specialise in specific sectors, such as utilities, financial firms, technology, or internet companies. Moreover, mutual funds can differ based on the types of instruments they invest in. Some focus solely on shares (equity funds), others on bonds (fixed income funds), while some are balanced funds, which invest in a mix of both equities and bonds. What is the usefulness of investing in mutual funds? Mutual funds allow investors to achieve broad diversification even with relatively small amounts of money. For example, instead of investing a large sum across 20 different individual securities, you can invest as little as €1,000 (or even less) in a mutual fund and gain exposure to a diversified portfolio. This reduces individual asset risk without requiring substantial wealth. Mutual funds are managed by experienced professionals who possess strong financial skills and extensive knowledge of the markets. These fund managers make informed investment decisions on behalf of investors, based on rigorous analysis and years of expertise. If you invest in an open-end mutual fund (which is the most common type), you can easily redeem your investment. There is no need to sell your shares on a secondary market. You simply request repayment directly from the fund management company, which will repurchase your shares at the current net asset value (NAV). This makes it straightforward to access your capital when needed. A pension fund operates differently from mutual funds. It is a fund in which money is collected from both employees and employers during the employee’s working life. The purpose of the fund is to provide financial support to the worker after retirement. At the end of their working life, the worker can either receive a lump-sum payment (liquidation of the investment) or a series of regular payments (a pension) drawn from their accumulated fund. Pension funds are not risk-free investments. The fund managers invest the collected money in financial markets, and therefore, the value of the pension depends on the performance of those investments. The level of risk depends on the type of assets in which the fund is invested. As an investor, you often have the option to choose from a range of pension funds, each with different risk-return profiles—ranging from conservative (e.g., mostly bonds) to aggressive (e.g., mostly equities). Banks collect money mainly from depositors—individuals who place their savings in bank accounts. Using these funds, banks are able to provide financing to households, businesses, and even governments through loans and credit services. The role of financial markets and intermediaries Financial markets and intermediaries perform several key functions that are essential for the efficient operation of modern economies. Borrowing and Lending Financial intermediaries such as banks solve a fundamental problem in lending: the difficulty of matching lenders and borrowers. From the lender’s point of view (e.g. an investor with excess funds), it is not easy to find someone who needs to borrow the exact amount of money, at the right time, and under mutually agreeable conditions. Similarly, borrowers would struggle to find individual lenders who trust them and are willing to take the risk. Banks eliminate this problem by acting as intermediaries If you have excess cash, you can simply deposit it in a bank, and the bank will pay you interest. On the other hand, if someone needs to borrow money, they can apply for a loan from the bank. In this way, banks connect savers with borrowers, making the process efficient and fast. Additionally, banks bear the risk of default (insolvency). If you lend money directly to someone and they don’t repay you, the loss is yours. But when you deposit your money in a bank, the bank assumes the credit risk of lending that money out. Pooling Risk As we mentioned in the case of mutual funds, financial markets allow for risk diversification. By investing in a well-functioning financial market, you can select securities with low correlation and low individual variance, which enables you to build a diversified portfolio and reduce overall financial risk. This principle is commonly summarised by the phrase: “Don’t put all your eggs in one basket.” Information One of the defining features of efficient financial markets is that the prices of securities reflect all available information. In such markets, prices act as signals of a firm's quality and performance. If a firm is performing well and has strong fundamentals, its security prices will tend to rise. Conversely, poorly performing firms will see falling prices. This price mechanism guides investment decisions: rational investors tend to buy high-quality securities (with rising prices) and avoid low-quality ones (with declining prices). For this reason, the investment bank usually interviews potential large investors—those likely to purchase substantial quantities of shares—to assess demand and price expectations. When setting the issue price, a common phenomenon arises: under-pricing. This refers to the tendency for newly issued shares—whether it's the first issue, second, third, or subsequent ones—to be offered at a price lower than what they might achieve in the open market. One reason for under-pricing is related to the investment bank’s role. Since the investment bank often agrees to underwrite any unsold shares, it reduces its risk by lowering the offering price. A lower price increases the likelihood that all shares will be sold. This strategy also aligns with the interests of the issuing firm. It is in the firm’s best interest to ensure the IPO is successful and fully subscribed. Therefore, by offering shares at a lower price, they increase the chances of attracting investors and successfully placing all shares. In cases where financial markets are inefficient or not well-functioning, the information reflected in share prices may not accurately represent the issuing firm’s true value. This can lead to asymmetric information—a situation where the firm knows more than potential investors. As a result, cautious investors may only be willing to buy shares at a lower price to compensate for the risk of not having full or accurate information. There are costs associated with an Initial Public Offering (IPO), which partly arise from administrative expenses and from fees paid to remunerate the consultancy services of the investment bank. Additionally, there is the cost of under-pricing. All these contribute to the overall cost of an IPO. Once the IPO has been successfully completed, new issues of shares can be sold to the general public in the domestic market. This method is referred to as a general cash offer, meaning that anyone—whether an existing shareholder or a new investor—has the right to purchase the newly issued shares. Alternatively, rights issues refer to the issuance of new shares where existing shareholders are given priority. They are granted the privilege to subscribe to the new shares before they are offered to new investors. Corporate Finance 106 Public issues and private placements Firms with shares already trading in financial markets can issue new shares either publicly in the financial markets or privately. Privately means selecting one or more specific investors to whom the new shares are offered. It is understood that in the case of non-listed firms, the only possible method for issuing new shares is through private placement. Agency problems and corporate governance Agency problems between managers and shareholders. The goal of corporate finance should be the maximisation of shareholders’ value or wealth, and this objective should be shared by everyone in the firm—from the CEO to workers on the assembly line. The relationship between managers and shareholders is known as an agency relationship, where managers act as agents for the shareholders, who are the principals. Managers are expected to act in the best interest of shareholders by making decisions that increase shareholder value. When managers fail to do so and instead act in their own interest, this creates what is known as an agency problem or agency conflict. When managers behave in ways that do not align with the maximisation of shareholder value, it means they may be extracting value from shareholders for their own personal benefit. For instance, a manager might reduce the effort devoted to the firm. perhaps spending work hours playing video games or planning their summer holidays instead of focusing on company tasks. While it is relatively easy to verify whether a manager is physically present at the workplace, it is much harder to measure the intensity and quality of their effort. For example, a manager might frequently go to the coffee machine, but how can we be sure that the remaining time is spent on value-creating activities like net present value (NPV) analysis? Another issue involves perks or private benefits. These are non-wage benefits typically enjoyed by managers, such as a company car, private helicopter, or even access to a personal aircraft. While such perks can be justified when they support business efficiency—for example, enabling fast travel to important meetings—they are sometimes used in a selfish or opportunistic way, bringing personal gain rather than contributing to shareholder value. Empire building, also known as overinvestment In large firms, the managerial structure tends to expand with multiple levels—middle managers, toplevel managers, a Chief Financial Officer (CFO), and possibly even a Chief Executive Officer (CEO). Naturally, in very small firms, all of these roles may be performed by a single person. In such cases, there is little opportunity for individual managers to improve their financial or reputational standing through promotions or role differentiation. As a result, an opportunistic manager might attempt to increase the size of the company not necessarily to maximise shareholder value, but rather to create more managerial positions. This expansion increases their chances for career progression and higher salaries, even if the growth is not justified by sound economic logic. An entrenching investment is a type of investment in a sector or business that a manager knows particularly well. This provides two main advantages for the manager: Reduced risk of failure, as the manager is highly familiar with the business environment and dynamics. Limited external scrutiny, since the manager's specialised knowledge may prevent others— such as shareholders or board members—from properly evaluating the investment decisions. Corporate Finance 107 However, from the shareholders' perspective, entrenching investments can be problematic. While they may reduce risk, they also reduce potential returns. Remember the fundamental principle: higher risk is usually associated with higher potential return. This does not mean that all investments should be high-risk, but consistently choosing only lowrisk, entrenching investments will eventually destroy shareholder value, as the manager will likely pursue projects with low or even negative Net Present Value (NPV). Risk taking or the «quiet life» approach and short-termism Why should I, as a manager, take risks if I can live a quiet life? I might prefer to invest only in more or less riskless projects. This way, I avoid taking any personal risks and reduce the chance of being fired. But who cares? You, the shareholder, care. This is because, once again, the financial manager is not fulfilling their duties properly. By only investing in low-risk projects, the manager is avoiding responsibility rather than acting in the best interests of the shareholders. This leads to value destruction over time. Another related concept is short-termism. This occurs when a manager focuses on investments that offer quick returns—projects with a very short recovery period—because this reduces the perceived risk of failure in the short term. Managers are compensated based on financial indicators, such as Return on Capital or Economic Value Added. While these indicators can be useful and objective to some extent, they are also partially under managerial control. For instance, a manager can improve these metrics not by enhancing the real performance of the firm, but simply by cutting costs. To increase Return on Equity, a manager might decide to reduce research and development expenses or cut back on employee training programmes. Although this leads to short-term improvements in financial indicators, it harms the company’s long-term value. Without sustained investment in R&D, the firm’s competitive position deteriorates over time. Likewise, if employee skills are not regularly updated, the firm may lose market share as competitors with better-trained staff become more productive and innovative. How is it possible to reduce agency conflicts between managers and shareholders? Typically, a "stick and carrot" approach is adopted. The "stick" refers to control mechanisms, primarily through monitoring the business. In small or medium enterprises, shareholder control over managers is relatively straightforward. Now, who is responsible for monitoring? Primarily, it is the shareholders. However, we must distinguish between small or medium-sized companies and large multinational corporations. There is often a close relationship between shareholders and managers. In many cases, shareholders are blockholders—that is, they own a significant portion of the company’s shares. The proximity between shareholders and managers facilitates effective oversight. Shareholders can directly question managers and hold them accountable In contrast, in large multinational companies, ownership is typically dispersed among many shareholders, each holding only a small percentage of shares. These shareholders often reside in different countries from where the company or its headquarters is located, making direct monitoring impractical. In such cases, the responsibility for oversight is delegated to the board of directors. The board of directors is composed of individuals who are nominated and dismissed by the shareholders. One might think, “Well, that’s fine—once the board of directors is appointed, shareholders are safe, and shareholder value will be maximised.” Not quite. That is not always the case, as there are several issues that can compromise the effectiveness of the board of directors. One key issue is independence. Corporate Finance 108 It is quite common for some board members to also be company managers—for example, the Chief Executive Officer or the Chief Financial Officer. This can be both appropriate and beneficial, as these individuals are responsible for the company’s main strategic decisions. However, having managers on the board is not inherently good or bad. Independence, in this context, refers to achieving a proper balance between executive directors (who are also managers) and non-executive directors (who are not involved in dayto-day management). A well-functioning board requires this balance. Of course, a manager cannot be considered an independent director Managers are appointed—and, when necessary, dismissed—by the board of directors, which makes their position inherently dependent. An independent director, on the other hand, is someone who has no personal or business relationship with the company, is not related to any of the managers, and is not a manager themselves. board size The board of directors is responsible for a wide range of tasks, as it must determine the overall strategy of the firm in areas such as production, distribution, organisational structure, and human resource management. Given the breadth of these responsibilities, it might seem that the larger the board, the better, since more members can be assigned to oversee specific functions and dedicate adequate time to each. However, a board that is too small—comprising only two or three individuals—may be overburdened, with a limited capacity to manage all these complex tasks effectively over-boarding Overboarding refers to situations where the same individuals serve on multiple boards— sometimes two, three, or even more. When directors sit on too many boards, the amount of time and attention they can devote to each firm is naturally limited. As a result, their effectiveness in overseeing company affairs may decline. To address this issue, some legal systems impose limits on the number of directorships an individual can hold simultaneously, in order to ensure that board members are able to perform their duties properly. election The election of board members is sometimes held annually, meaning there is a yearly renewal of votes. However, in some cases, only part of the board is re-elected every three years. Over time, board members who stay for longer periods can develop closer relationships with the managers. They may form friendships or even become colleagues, potentially compromising their objectivity and ability to effectively monitor the company's activities. Therefore, it is generally a good idea to hold elections for board members at least once a year to maintain fresh perspectives and ensure accountability. Auditors Auditors are external organisations or firms that review a company's accounting records as well as its overall economic and financial situation. In many cases, auditing is compulsory by law, with regulations requiring companies to undergo regular audits. These periodic reviews serve as a form of external control over management. Knowing they are subject to audits, managers are more likely to act responsibly and perform well, as any signs of poor Corporate Finance 109 performance or opportunistic behaviour will be detected by auditors and reported to the board of directors. Lenders Lenders, such as banks, also serve as effective monitoring agents. No financial institution will lend money to a company unless it considers the firm trustworthy and financially stable. To do this, banks must assess financial risk thoroughly. In doing so, they indirectly evaluate the quality of the company’s financial management, including the performance of the board and the chief financial officer. Rival Companies Rival companies can act as monitoring agents, especially in the case of listed firms. When a company is listed on the stock exchange, it becomes susceptible to takeovers, including hostile takeovers—those not supported or initiated by the current shareholders or management. In such cases, the ownership of the firm can shift from the existing shareholders to new ones. It is relatively easy to acquire shares of a listed company, especially when the stock price is low, which often indicates poor performance. This creates an opportunity for other companies or investors to buy up shares and attempt a takeover. Therefore, the threat of hostile takeovers serves as a disciplinary mechanism for managers. To avoid losing control of the firm, it is in the managers' interest to perform well, maintain strong financial performance, and ensure high share prices, thereby making the company a less attractive target for hostile acquisition. The "carrot" refers to incentives, which aim to encourage managers to act in alignment with shareholders' interests. How can we incentivise managers to act in the best interests of shareholders? One common method is through compensation schemes. In fact, a large portion of managerial compensation is not fixed; rather, it is variable and performance-based. This variable component is often linked to financial indicators such as Return on Capital (ROC), Return on Equity (ROE), Return on Assets (ROA), or Economic Value Added (EVA). The advantage of using such ratios is that they are relatively easy to calculate, relying solely on accounting data taken from financial statements. However, the drawback is that the calculation and presentation of these figures are influenced by the managers themselves. For example, managers might inflate inventory levels, reduce amortisation, or cut training and research & development expenses in order to artificially boost short-term results. Another popular form of incentive is stock-based compensation, where a manager’s pay is tied to the company's share price performance—either through stock options or by awarding company shares directly. This aligns managerial interests with those of shareholders, as both parties benefit from rising stock prices. However, this method also has a downside: share prices are affected by broader market trends, not just the firm’s internal performance In a bull market, stock prices may rise regardless of managerial ability. Conversely, in a bear market, prices may fall even when a manager is highly competent and the firm is well-managed. Thus, a manager might be unfairly penalised or rewarded due to general market movements rather than their own performance. Stakeholder capitalism and responsible business The goal of a firm, traditionally, is the maximisation of shareholder value. This is considered the classical capitalist objective of a firm: to act in the interests of its owners by increasing their wealth. Corporate Finance 110 According to this view, maximising shareholder value also indirectly serves the interests of other stakeholders—such as suppliers, customers, employees, managers, and even the government—by ensuring the firm remains profitable, competitive, and sustainable. This traditional approach was famously advocated by Milton Friedman, who wrote a widely cited article in 1970. In it, he stated that "the business of business is business", meaning that a company’s sole responsibility is to maximise profits, provided it respects the law and the rules of fair competition. Under this view, firms are not expected to pursue social or environmental goals unless doing so directly contributes to profit generation. One of the strengths of the shareholder value approach is its clarity. It offers a clear decision-making rule that can guide everyone within the organisation—from the CEO to workers. If the firm is listed on the financial market, this goal becomes even more transparent: maximising shareholder value simply means ensuring that share prices rise as much as possible. However, Milton Friedman argued that shareholder value maximisation—the foundation of shareholder capitalism—is appropriate only under three key conditions that support this approach. The first condition is that government policies must ensure that companies behave in a socially responsible manner. In his 1970 article, Friedman explained that democratically elected governments are responsible for implementing the electoral programmes for which they have received a mandate from the voters. These programmes are then translated into laws and regulations, which companies are expected to follow. As long as firms comply with these rules, they are considered to be acting appropriately. Friedman argued that it is not the role of companies to decide what is socially or ethically right; instead, this is the role of the government, acting on behalf of the public. A company’s duty is to maximise profits, provided it operates within the legal and regulatory framework defined by the state. He illustrates this with the example of smoking In most countries, smoking is not completely prohibited, despite its health risks. Rather, governments typically impose taxes on tobacco products as a means of discouraging consumption. This reflects a democratic compromise: rather than banning smoking outright—which would dissatisfy both smokers and cigarette producers—governments use fiscal tools to reduce consumption. According to Friedman, this approach demonstrates how governments can balance public health concerns with personal and economic freedoms. Therefore, Friedman concludes that as long as a company respects the legal rules established by the government, it is not required to take on broader social responsibilities Maximising shareholder value allows investors to pursue social objectives. This means that companies should not engage in charitable activities According to Friedman, the role of a firm is not to decide how profits should be used for social causes. Instead, its responsibility is to maximise shareholder wealth, thereby giving individual shareholders the freedom to support the causes they personally value. For example, suppose a company is considering whether to donate to a cancer research organisation or to an environmental initiative fighting climate change. These are both worthy causes, but there is no reason the firm itself should decide between them. Instead, by distributing profits to shareholders, each individual investor can independently choose which cause to support—whether it’s cancer research, environmental protection, or something else entirely. In short, maximising shareholder value enhances personal freedom, allowing shareholders to allocate their wealth according to their own ethical and social preferences. This avoids the problem of a company making moral or political decisions on behalf of its investors and ensures that business remains focused on its primary economic function Corporate Finance 111 Maximising shareholder value requires companies to invest in stakeholders. This means that by focusing on shareholder value maximisation, a firm also indirectly creates value for other stakeholders. Shareholders hold a residual claim on the firm’s cash flows—meaning they receive what is left after all other obligations to stakeholders (such as employees, suppliers, and creditors) have been met. Therefore, the greater the total cash flow generated by the firm, the greater the potential value available not only to shareholders but also to stakeholder In order to maximise shareholder value, companies must ensure that stakeholders remain engaged and satisfied. If customers are dissatisfied, they will stop buying the company’s products. If employees are poorly treated, they may leave or underperform. If banks and investors do not trust the firm, they will stop financing it. Thus, a firm cannot achieve sustainable shareholder value without also investing in relationships with stakeholders. There are several criticisms of the three conditions outlined in support of shareholder value maximisation. First, even in democratically elected governments, problems can arise. Lobbying—the influence exerted by powerful groups or individuals—can distort the policymaking process. In many cases, the electoral programmes through which governments are elected are heavily influenced by lobbying groups, and thus may reflect special interests rather than the general will of the electorate. Second, politicians are often primarily concerned with re-election. The most significant fear for many members of parliament is not being re-elected. As a result, when their mandate is nearing its end, politicians may be reluctant to introduce unpopular legislation, even if it is necessary. For instance, they may postpone implementing new taxes to avoid upsetting voters close to an election. Typically, new taxation measures are introduced at the beginning of a government’s term, when political capital is strongest. As the end of the mandate approaches, the likelihood of passing unpopular measures decreases, as politicians prioritise electoral approval over long-term policy objectives. Thirdly, when it comes to particularly sensitive and complex issues—such as immigration in Europe or decisions about end-of-life care for terminally ill individuals—it is difficult to translate general ethical or moral intentions into concrete legislation These issues are deeply divisive and often resist simple regulatory solutions. For all these reasons, even democratically elected governments may not be the most effective or reliable mechanism for ensuring responsible corporate behaviour under a shareholder value maximisation model. Another criticism relates to the lack of comparative advantage for companies in relying on shareholders to serve broader social interests. According to the shareholder value approach, firms should avoid engaging in charitable or socially responsible activities and should instead maximise profits, leaving it to individual shareholders to decide how to allocate their wealth. However, this can be impractical. For example, suppose a group of shareholders wants the company to replace plastic packaging with a more sustainable alternative, such as cardboard. In theory, these shareholders could fund a lobbying group to pressure the government into banning plastic packaging. But this route is expensive, time-consuming, and uncertain in terms of outcome. On the other hand, the company itself could make the switch directly and efficiently, without needing new legislation. Corporate Finance 112 In such cases, it may actually be cheaper, faster, and more effective for a company to adopt socially responsible practices on its own, rather than relying on shareholders and the political process to bring about the same change. Maximising shareholders’ value does not always mean maximising the value and satisfaction of other stakeholders Workers and employees often worry about who will take care of their children during working hours. Suppose a company is considering building a day-care facility where employees can leave their children while they work. If such a facility were available near the workplace, employees would be much more satisfied and at ease, knowing that their children are being well cared for nearby. At the end of the working day, they could pick them up and go home peacefully. This would likely improve their morale and potentially even their productivity. Now, what must the company do before deciding whether to build the day-care facility? Capital budgeting, it needs to conduct an assessment. That means evaluating the cash flows, both inflows and outflows, associated with the project. The outflows are relatively easy to estimate—they include the initial investment, the cost of managing the day care, staff salaries, equipment, food, toys, etc. However, estimating the inflows is much more difficult You might say that inflows are represented by the opportunity cost savings —i.e., not having to pay for private childcare elsewhere. That’s a fair point, but from the firm’s perspective, it is interested in its own inflows'. So, what could the firm possibly gain? One potential inflow is the increased productivity of employees, thanks to their greater job satisfaction and reduced stress. But this is extremely difficult to measure. Because the costs are predictable and the benefits are uncertain, in many cases, the company may decide not to build the day care facility. This illustrates a situation where shareholder value maximisation does not automatically lead to stakeholder value maximisation. As a consequence, over time, a new approach was developed. This is known as stakeholder capitalism, or stakeholder value maximisation. However, this approach is more of a philosophy than a practical framework. Why? Because it raises a fundamental question: How can a firm possibly address the interests of all stakeholders simultaneously? In reality, these interests often conflict with one another. For example, what benefits employees (e.g., higher wages, better benefits) might reduce profits for shareholders. What pleases customers (e.g., lower prices) might limit resources for innovation or sustainability. These tensions make it extremely difficult to identify a clear, consistent objective. If you ask a manager to define a goal that satisfies all stakeholders, after a few days of reflection they might feel like they’re going mad—because in practice, it’s nearly impossible to implement such a vague and conflicting set of targets. Stakeholder capitalism has paved the way for a more recent and ambitious approach known as responsible business, responsible capitalism, or socially responsible capitalism. The idea behind responsible business is that a firm’s activity should be oriented towards the well-being of society as a whole. This includes caring for the community, the workers, and the environment, aiming to serve broader social interests—not just those of the shareholders. This approach is supported by three key principles principle of multiplication; Corporate Finance 113 It means that the social benefits of a given action must significantly outweigh the associated costs in order for the action to be considered socially responsible. Imagine your company has the option to install a new plant that would reduce emissions by just 1%. You might decide not to go ahead with that investment, because the costs of the new plant are much higher than the very modest environmental benefit it would bring. In such a case, the principle of multiplication is not satisfied. Comparative Advantage; When undertaking a socially responsible activity, a firm should ideally possess a comparative advantage over its competitors in carrying out that activity. During the pandemic, some industrial companies converted their production lines to manufacture medical devices such as masks and ventilators to support public health efforts. For some companies, this conversion was relatively easy and efficient—they had the necessary infrastructure, skills, or technology in place. These firms had a comparative advantage, which allowed them to continue operating and maintain sales levels, even during the crisis. Other firms, lacking this advantage, found it much harder—or even impossible—to adapt. Many of them were forced to shut down, unless they received financial support from their governments. Principle of Materiality The principle of materiality states that the implementation of socially responsible activities ultimately leads to the satisfaction of all stakeholders, including shareholders. In other words, by protecting workers' conditions, respecting the community in which the firm operates, and safeguarding the environment, the firm also increases value for its shareholders. Today, for example, a company might choose to invest heavily to transform its production processes into ones that are healthier and more environmentally sustainable. But how is this type of investment profitable? It is profitable because, in the long run, such efforts tend to be rewarded by investors, consumers, and the market as a whole. People’s attitudes are changing: consumers are increasingly drawn to responsible businesses, and investors are more willing to fund companies that demonstrate a sustainable and ethical approach to business What is the age of responsible business? The current frontier. Today, the frontier of responsible business is defined by the ESG framework, which stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. This concept reflects the modern understanding of sustainable management. E stands for Environmental: Business activities must be oriented towards the protection of the environment, including reducing emissions, managing waste, and using resources responsibly. S stands for Social: Firms must also protect and support the communities in which they operate, ensuring socially responsible activities that benefit workers, consumers, and society at large. G stands for Governance: This refers to having a corporate structure that is fair, transparent, and accountable. It includes the establishment of just and ethical relationships among all stakeholders— shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, and the board. A bond is the most common form of long-term financing issued by a firm. Corporate Finance 114 According to the bond contract, the bondholder has the right to receive periodic interest payments and, at maturity, the repayment of the principal originally provided. The bond contract contains a series of clauses that define and regulate the relationship between the bondholders and the issuing firm. The maturity date (or expiry date) of the bond. The interest rate, which may be fixed or floating. The face value (also known as par value or nominal value) of the bond, which is the amount to be repaid at maturity. It is important to note that the issue price of the bond can differ from its face value. For example, the issuing company may receive only 90 for a bond with a face value of 100. A secured bond is therefore considered privileged in terms of repayment, because only the holders of these bonds have the right to benefit from the capital obtained through the sale of the asset set aside as collateral. Asset-backed securities Asset-backed securities (ABS) are a type of bond issued in the context of securitisation processes. They are used to transform illiquid assets into liquid, tradable securities. Consider the case of a bank that holds numerous mortgage loans A mortgage loan is a form of credit extended to individuals or families who want to buy a house but do not have sufficient funds. The bank provides the capital, and the borrower repays it over time. These mortgage loans are assets for the bank, as they represent money to be received in the future. The bank has two options: Hold these loans until maturity, collecting repayments over time; or Liquidate them before maturity through a securitisation process. To do this, the bank creates a separate entity known as a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) The SPV is a company set up exclusively to carry out the securitisation transaction. The SPV needs funds to purchase these loans from the bank, so it issues bonds on the financial markets. These bonds are called Asset-Backed Securities (ABS). Corporate Finance 115 3. Investors buy these ABS, providing the SPV with the necessary capital to pay the bank for the mortgage loans. These ABS are called “asset-backed” because they are secured by the underlying assets—in this case, the mortgage loans. The cash flows from the mortgages (i.e., the repayments from homeowners) are used by the SPV to repay the bondholders. However, there is risk involved. If the borrowers (the homeowners) fail to repay their mortgages, the SPV may be unable to meet its obligations to the bondholders. Therefore, the quality of the underlying credit assets is crucial to the value and safety of the asset-backed securities. Call Provision A call provision is a right held by the issuing firm that allows it to repay its bonds earlier than the agreed maturity date. For example, suppose a bond contract has a maturity of 10 years. With a call provision, the company has the option to repay the bonds before the 10-year term ends. Why would a firm choose to repay its bonds early? One common reason is changes in market interest rates. Suppose a company issues bonds at a fixed interest rate during a period when interest rates are relatively high. If, over time, general interest rates decline, the firm might find it financially advantageous to repay its existing bonds early and refinance by issuing new bonds at a lower interest rate. This strategy reduces the overall cost of borrowing. In short, a call provision enables the issuing firm to reduce interest expenses over time by taking advantage of favourable changes in market interest rates. Sinking Fund A sinking fund is a method of repaying bonds that serves as an alternative to a call provision. In this case, it is up to the issuing firm to decide whether to repay bondholders by either: repaying the face (nominal) value of the bonds, or repurchasing the bonds on the open market. The decision between these two options is primarily financial. If the market value of the bonds is higher than the nominal value, the firm will choose to repay the nominal value. Conversely, if the market value is lower than the nominal value, the firm will repurchase the bonds on the market at the lower price. Bond Covenants Bond covenants are clauses within a bond contract that impose certain restrictions on the issuing firm. These clauses are designed to enhance the likelihood of successful repayment and protect the interests of bondholders. Typical restrictions may include limits on: the distribution of future dividends, the issuance of new debt, or the issuance of debt with higher seniority. Convertible Bonds A convertible bond is a type of bond that includes an embedded option in the bond contract. This option is held by the bondholder, allowing them to convert their position from a debtholder into a shareholder. From the issuer’s perspective, the advantage lies in retaining the invested capital and converting debt into equity, which strengthens the firm's capital structure. From the bondholder’s perspective, the main benefit is flexibility. During the conversion window, the bondholder can assess the firm’s performance and potential. If the company proves to be strong and promising, the bondholder may opt to convert the bond into shares and benefit from future growth. If the company underperforms, the bondholder can simply choose to be repaid the initial capital at maturity, thus limiting their downside risk. In contrast, the conversion option in a convertible bond cannot be sold separately—the bondholder must either exercise the option or let it expire. Additionally, tax treatment may differ between convertible bonds and bonds with warrants, as tax authorities often treat the two instruments differently. Longevity Bonds Longevity bonds are a type of bond where interest payments are reduced or eliminated if mortality rates decrease—that is, if people live longer than expected. What kind of organisations would find longevity bonds useful? They are particularly useful for pension funds and life annuity providers. These institutions make regular payments to individuals who have retired. If retirees live longer, the funds must continue making payments for a longer period, which increases their liabilities. By issuing longevity bonds, these institutions can partly offset the cost of increased longevity. If people live longer, and the issuer is paying more to retirees, they compensate for that financial pressure by paying less interest to the holders of the longevity bonds. Mortality Bonds Mortality bonds are bonds that pay reduced interest or no interest at all if mortality rates rise —that is, if more people die than expected, based on statistical projections. These bonds are primarily used by insurance companies, particularly life insurers. Here’s how they work: Insurance companies collect premiums from policyholders and, in the event of a policyholder’s death, are obliged to pay a predetermined amount to the beneficiary (usually a relative of the deceased). If mortality rates increase significantly—due to a pandemic, natural disaster, or other largescale event—the insurance company faces a sharp rise in payouts. Mortality bonds help mitigate this risk. In such cases, the insurer can reduce or suspend interest payments to investors, easing the financial burden of increased death benefit obligations. Bank Loans Bank loans are a traditional form of bank financing, whereby a bank provides a specific amount of money or financial resources to a borrower. These loans can take the form of either short-term or long-term financing. While they are typically short-term, loans lasting two or more years are also common. Bank loans are often extended through a commitment, meaning the borrower has access to a line of credit up to a specified limit. The firm can draw on this amount as needed, up to the agreed-upon maximum. The loan contract always specifies a maturity date, which marks the end of the loan period. However, many bank loans are revolving, meaning they can be renewed repeatedly—unless either the bank or the borrower decides to terminate the financing relationship. Once the maturity date is reached, the contract is typically renewed automatically, unless notice is given by either party to discontinue the agreement. As with any form of debt, interest is charged on bank loans. This interest rate may be either fixed or floating. The higher the borrower's risk, the higher the interest rate charged by the bank. Syndicated Loans Syndicated loans are loans provided by a group (or syndicate) of banks rather than a single lending institution. This structure is typically used when the amount to be borrowed is too large for any one bank to finance alone. In such cases, multiple banks pool their resources to fund a single loan. This allows them to share both the risk and the returns associated with lending a significant sum, often used to finance large-scale investments or projects. Security (Collateral) Security, in the context of loans, refers to whether the loan is secured or unsecured. Leasing Leasing is a form of financing that involves a contract between two parties: the owner of an asset and its user. Under this agreement, the user is allowed to use the asset in exchange for the payment of a series of instalments over a specified period. At the end of the lease contract, the user may have the right to purchase the asset, such as equipment or machinery. In some cases, the owner is also the manufacturer of the asset. If not, a third party called a leasing company is involved. The leasing company buys the asset from the manufacturer and leases it to the user. There are several types of lease contracts, but a common distinction is between operating leases This is a short-term lease, often lasting only a few months or less. Typically, the asset is not purchased at the end of the lease period. Operating leases are often used for temporary needs, and maintenance and risks related to the asset are usually borne by the owner. financial leases This is a longer-term lease, potentially lasting several years. It usually includes a clause allowing the user to purchase the asset at the end of the lease term. In this case, the user bears most of the risks and rewards of ownership, and the lease often covers the full useful life of the asset. The leasing operation is tied to a specific source of financing — the lease is "leveraged" because it relies heavily on debt. It allows the leasing company to acquire expensive assets without using too much of its own capital, and to pass the cost and risk structure to the lessee through the lease payments.
