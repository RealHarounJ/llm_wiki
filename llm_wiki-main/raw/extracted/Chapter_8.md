The equation of the security market line, which is the capital asset pricing model, holds for all assets Validity and role of the Capital Asset Pricing Model The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is widely followed by both investors and financial managers. In particular, financial managers use it when calculating the present value of future investment projects. In this respect, the CAPM is especially useful in capital budgeting decisions. One key reason for the model’s success is its simplicity. The formula is easy to apply, yet it captures the essential trade-off between risk and return. However, whether the CAPM is truly useful in the real world depends on how well it actually predicts expected returns on stocks. That’s the crucial point. The model focuses only on systematic risk, which is the portion of risk that cannot be diversified away. This is a strength, as it reflects the no-free-lunch principle in finance: investors should only be rewarded for bearing risk that cannot be eliminated through diversification. Basically, we consider the risk premium, which is the return an investor requires to be encouraged to invest in a risky security. This risk premium is only associated with systematic risk, because specific (or unsystematic) risk can be diversified away. The tests of the CAPM Researchers have found that the relationship between betas and expected returns is flatter than what is predicted by the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). In other words, when plotting betas against returns, the observed relationship — based on empirical data — is not as steep as the one suggested by the Security Market Line (SML). The dots on the graph represent various combinations of betas and actual returns over a long period. Many of these combinations lie along a flatter line than predicted by CAPM. Why does this happen? There is no single answer. One reason could be that CAPM deals with expected returns, whereas researchers are using historical data — that is, past returns and past beta estimates — which may not perfectly align with future expectations. This suggests there may be a dimension of risk associated with smaller firms that CAPM does not capture. All differences in expected return come from differences in beta (i.e., exposure to systematic market risk). So if two firms have the same beta, CAPM says they should have the same expected return, regardless of size. Empirical data shows: Smaller firms (low market cap) tend to have higher average returns than larger firms… …even when their betas are the same or lower. A further issue is the observation that companies with a high book-to-market ratio (i.e., value stocks) also tend to exhibit higher average returns. The book value refers to the equity value derived from a company's balance sheet, while the market value is its current market capitalisation. CAPM does not account for this distinction either, leaving it as another unexplained critical point. limitations of the CAPM The assumption of a completely risk-free return is unrealistic. In the real world, a truly risk-free asset does not exist. Even government securities, often used as proxies, carry some degree of inflation or reinvestment risk. Borrowing and lending rates are not the same. CAPM assumes that investors can borrow and lend at the same risk-free rate. However, in practice, the rate at which investors can borrow is higher than the rate at which they can lend. This discrepancy is known as the difference between active and passive interest rates. For example, a bank pays a lower rate on deposits (passive rate) than it charges on loans (active rate). This fundamental asymmetry undermines the model's assumption Limited investment opportunities. CAPM assumes that investors are willing to invest solely in a combination of the risk-free asset and the market portfolio. In reality, investors may diversify beyond this simple two-asset framework, considering alternative investments such as real estate, private equity, or international assets, which are not accounted for in the model. Some alternative theories to the CAPM Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT) The APT approach considers multiple potential sources of market risk, referred to as factors (e.g., Factor 1, Factor 2, Factor 3, etc.). In the APT formula, each of these factors is multiplied by a corresponding beta, which represents the sensitivity of a particular asset to that specific type of systematic risk. These factors can include events such as changes in inflation, interest rates, oil and gas prices, political assassinations, or broader geopolitical events. The main difference between CAPM and APT lies in how systematic risk is treated. CAPM simplifies systematic risk into a single factor: the market risk. APT, on the other hand, breaks systematic risk into multiple components, each representing a different possible economic or political influence. As a result, APT provides a more detailed analysis of systematic risk. However, this detail comes at a cost: it requires the identification and estimation of specific factors, which is often difficult and subject to forecasting uncertainty. The APT agrees with the CAPM that there are two sources of risk for an individual stock. The first is the risks specific to the company.
