This suggests a liquidity problem because the company might struggle to meet its short-term obligations if they all came due at once. For a situation to be in equilibrium the current ratio should be greater than one. The current ratio is essentially a representation of net working capital in ratio form. If the net working capital is positive, then the current ratio in equilibrium should logically be greater than one. Cash ratio A company’s most liquid assets are its holdings of cash and marketable securities. That is why analysts also look at the cash ratio: The cash ratio, which further removes receivables from the numerator, can be less than one but should not deviate too far from this threshold. A low cash ratio might not be a problem if the company can quickly borrow on short notice. It doesn't matter whether the company has already borrowed or if it has a line of credit that lets it borrow anytime. Standard liquidity measures don't consider this ability to borrow on reserve. Managers also focus on liquid assets because their recorded values are more reliable. While the value of equipment or property can be uncertain, cash in the bank always has a clear and definite worth. Liquidity ratios have some drawbacks. Since short-term assets and liabilities change quickly, these measures can become outdated fast. While the value of equipment may be uncertain, it remains relatively stable, whereas cash in the bank can be spent instantly. Too much liquidity is not always a good thing. Efficient firms do not leave excess cash in their bank accounts. They don’t allow customers to postpone paying their bills, and they don’t leave stocks of raw materials and finished goods littering the warehouse floor. In other words, high levels of liquidity may indicate sloppy use of capital. EVA can help because it penalises managers who keep more liquid assets than they really need.
