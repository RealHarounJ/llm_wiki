Corporate Finance Introduction to Corporate Finance (Chapter 1) Corporate finance deals with the financial decisions and policies of a firm. The term "corporate" refers to firms and enterprises, while "finance" pertains to financial decision-making. The main financial decisions of a firm fall into three categories: Investment Decisions Investment decisions involve the allocation of a firm's financial resources. Corporations invest in real assets (tangible and intangible), which generate income. This includes purchasing products, raw materials, equipment, and machinery, income from patent, copyright, trademarks, R&D, etc. choosing projects, assets, or ventures that can generate future growth and profitability. as well as covering expenses such as wages, advertising, and sales. Essentially, any use of capital within a firm qualifies as an investment decision. A financial manager employs financial resources with the expectation of recovering them, along with an additional return, to generate value for the firm. Financial managers know (or quickly learn) that cash returns are not guaranteed. An investment could be a smashing success or a dismal failure. The real value of a company stems from its investment decisions—what makes a company great is what it does (its investment decisions) rather than how it pays for it (its financing decisions). These decisions matter more on the upside because they determine how well a firm can expand, innovate, and create value for shareholders. A good investment decision can lead to high returns, competitive advantages, and long-term success. Financing Decisions The financing decision includes not just raising cash today but also meeting its obligations to banks, bondholders, and shareholders that have contributed financing in the past. Financing decisions mainly focus on how a firm raises the money needed for investments. The firm can obtain funds through: Corporate Finance 1 Equity: Financial resources provided by shareholders, i.e., the owners of the firm. The shareholders are equity investors, who contribute equity financing. Debt: Borrowed funds from banks, bondholders, or other financial institutions. The choice between debt and equity financing is called the capital structure decision. Capital refers to the firm’s sources of long-term financing. Corporations raise equity financing in two ways. First, they can issue new shares of stock The investors who buy the new shares put up cash in exchange for a fraction of the corporation’s future cash flow and profits. Second, the corporation can take the cash flow generated by its existing assets and reinvest that cash in new assets. In this case the corporation is reinvesting on behalf of existing shareholders. Whenever a manager reinvests cash, she’s choosing not to pay out that cash to shareholders— she’s effectively raising money from shareholders. When a corporation does not reinvest all of the clash flow generated it existing asset, it may hold the cash in reserve for future investment, or it may pay the cash back to its shareholders. The decision to pay dividends or repurchase shares is called the pay-out decision. Financial decisions relate to capital structure, debt management, and liquidity—essentially, how a company funds its investments and manages financial risk. These decisions are particularly important on the downside because they determine how well a company can survive during downturns, crises, or periods of financial distress. Pay-out Decisions Once a financial manager has invested money in the firm and generated returns—either by utilising assets or selling products and services—the next step is to distribute the recovered funds. These funds must first be used to repay debt holders, as the firm is legally obligated to return their capital. However, the distribution of income to shareholders is a discretionary financial decision. The amount returned to shareholders depends on a decision made either by the shareholders themselves or by the board of directors, depending on the legal system in place. The different types of firms Regardless of a firm's characteristics, the principles of corporate finance apply to all businesses. The definition and concepts of corporate finance are relevant to any type of company, regardless of size or industry. Limited Liability Company (LLC) A limited liability company is a business structure in which the owners (shareholders) are only responsible for the firm's debts up to the amount they have invested. This means that if the firm's debts exceed the invested capital, shareholders cannot be required to cover the remaining liabilities with their personal assets. Unlimited Liability Company In contrast, an unlimited liability company is a business structure where owners face a higher financial risk. If the company encounters financial distress, shareholders are liable not only for the amount they have invested but also for any outstanding debts beyond that. In such cases, they may be required to use their personal assets to cover the firm's liabilities. What Is a Corporation? A corporation is a legal entity. In the view of the law, it is a legal person that is owned by its shareholders. As a legal person, the corporation can make contracts, carry on a business, borrow or lend money, and sue or be sued. It must also pay taxes. Can buy another corporation. A corporation’s board of directors. The board is responsible for making major financial decisions, such as mergers and acquisitions, and for amending the company's statutory documents. Additionally, the board of directors plays a crucial role in overseeing top management. Shareholders can lose their entire investment in a corporation, but no more. When a corporation is first established, its shares may be privately held by a small group of investors, such as the company’s managers and a few backers. In this case, the shares are not publicly traded and the company is closely held. Eventually, when the firm grows and new shares are issued to raise additional capital, its shares are traded in public markets such as the New York Stock Exchange or Hong Kong Stock Exchange. These corporations are known as public companies. A large public corporation may have millions of shareholders, who own the business but cannot possibly manage or control it directly. This separation of ownership and control gives corporations permanence. Even if managers quit or are dismissed and replaced, the corporation survives. Today’s shareholders can sell all their shares to new investors without disrupting the operations of the business. On the other hand, the financial manager deals with investors—not just with shareholders but also with financial institutions such as banks and with financial markets such as the London Stock Exchange . Shareholders Want Managers to Maximise Market Value Major corporations may have millions of shareholders. Authority has to be delegated to professional managers, just as residents delegate the running of a city to the mayor. Every investor wants the financial manager to increase shareholder wealth. Shareholders have different interests but fortunately, for managers, there is a natural financial objective on which most shareholders can agree despite their differences: Maximise the current market value of shareholders’ investment in the firm. A smart and effective manager makes decisions that increase the current market value of the company’s shares. This increased market value can then be put to whatever purposes the shareholders want, inter alia, sell their shares or retain their entire investment in the firm. In a well-functioning financial market, is it a problem if a shareholder receives more or less than they initially expected? The answer depends on how efficiently financial markets operate If a shareholder receives more money than anticipated, there is no issue. They can simply reinvest the extra funds in the financial markets, selecting investments that align with their risk tolerance and desired return. In this case, the market provides alternative opportunities for investors to manage their excess returns efficiently. If a shareholder receives less than they expected, they have options as well. They can borrow money from financial markets or banks to cover the shortfall. Over time, they may recover their position by selling shares in the company, assuming that the financial manager has invested wisely and increased the firm's value. If financial management has made sound investment decisions, the company's share price should appreciate, allowing the shareholder to sell their shares at a profit and repay their debt. These examples illustrate that, in an efficient market, fluctuations in shareholder returns and preferences are not a fundamental problem. Financial markets allow shareholders to transport savings across time, by borrowing money or selling shares (if they wish to consume today) or lending money or buying more shares (if they prefer to consume in the future). Financial markets give investors the flexibility to manage their own savings and investment plans, leaving the corporation’s financial managers with only one task: to increase market value and hence shareholder wealth. Profit maximisation is not a well-defined financial objective for at least two reasons: Which year’s profits? A corporation may be able to increase current profits by cutting R&D, but that may result in lower profits in the future. What about risk? If a project will increase the company’s profits but also make it riskier, it is not clear that the manager should take it. Should Managers Maximise Shareholder Wealth? One challenge of maximising the “current” market value leads managers to be short-ermist—that is, focus on short-term profits by reducing investment or cutting wages. A second criticism is that it’s narrowly focused on shareholders at the expense of stakeholders. Corporate Finance 4 A single-minded focus on shareholders might cause managers to price-gouge customers, overwork employees, or pollute the environment in pursuit of shareholder wealth. However, these criticisms are not fully warranted Current shareholder wealth depends on all future cash flows generated by a company, not just current cash flows. A company developing renewable energy may be improving current shareholder wealth, even if the project is not profitable for 20 years. A company failing to train its workforce may be destroying shareholder value, even though the cost savings will boost short-term profits. Moving to the latter, it is not true that a focus on enriching the shareholders means that managers must act as greedy mercenaries riding roughshod over the weak and helpless. Investing in stakeholders often benefits shareholders, and any financial manager who fails to take these effects into account is failing at her job. Profitable firms are those with satisfied customers and loyal employees; firms with dissatisfied customers and a disgruntled workforce will probably end up with declining profits and a low stock price. Moreover, according to the shareholder value maximisation approach, by focusing on maximising shareholder wealth, a firm will inherently also maximise the value and satisfaction of all other stakeholders. In this view, profit is seen as a residual value. Only once all other stakeholders have been compensated —whether through wages, payments, services, or taxes—does the firm realise its profit. If a company generates a profit, it indicates that all stakeholders have been satisfied. It is important to acknowledge that the two preceding criticisms may sometimes be valid. Starting with the short-termism critique, We’ve been assuming that the stock market is efficient, meaning that a company's share price reflects all its future cash flows. But what if the stock market is short-sighted and ignores long-term cash flows? In that case, a company’s market value (stock price) wouldn’t truly reflect its shareholder value (overall worth to investors). This could lead to problems: A company making a smart, long-term investment might see its stock price drop unfairly. If managers fear this reaction, they might avoid good investments, even if those investments would benefit shareholders in the long run. A solution is to pay the manager in long-term shares, so that she is less concerned with the shortterm stock price. Though, companies can also be knocked off course by recessions, by changes in commodity prices, interest rates and exchange rates, or by adverse political developments. Let’s move to the second criticism, Unfortunately, some investments in stakeholders won’t fully feed back into shareholder value, even in the long term. If a company invests billions in reducing its greenhouse gas emissions, the benefits are enjoyed by many, but the company’s own share of the gains may be small. The consequences that companies exert on society, but don’t feed back into their profits, are known as externalities. A company that’s focused on shareholder wealth will ignore externalities and thus may turn down certain investments in stakeholders. As a result, managers’ objectives may need to be broadened for them to fully take stakeholder interests into account. The shareholder versus the stakeholder approach Corporate Finance 5 Traditionally, the correct goal for a company has been to maximise shareholder value. However, in recent years, alternative perspectives have gained prominence. Academics and professionals have recognised that focusing solely on shareholder value may not always align with the broader interests of all stakeholders. Such as employees, customers, suppliers, and society as a whole. This has led to the rise of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria as a framework for corporate decision-making. What is ESG? Environmental: A company’s impact on the environment, including sustainability practices and carbon footprint. Social: The company’s relationships with employees, customers, and communities. Governance: The internal systems and processes ensuring ethical business practices, transparency, and accountability. Under the ESG framework, the goal of a company extends beyond maximising shareholder value to satisfying the interests of society as a whole. That the hurdle rate is an opportunity cost of investing elsewhere should make it clear that the minimum required return for a project depends entirely on external factors—the rates of return that shareholders could obtain elsewhere. Managers look to the financial markets to measure the opportunity cost of capital for the firm’s investment projects. They can observe the opportunity cost of capital for safe investments by looking up current interest rates on safe debt securities. For risky investments, the opportunity cost of capital has to be estimated. A key question for financial managers is whether they are truly creating value for shareholders. If the company is publicly listed, share prices offer a more objective and reliable measure. In a well-functioning financial market, share prices reflect the perceived value of a company. If the company is a high-quality firm, investors will be willing to purchase its shares, driving up the price. This increase in share price is often interpreted as a sign that the financial manager has created value for shareholders. Conversely, if the market perceives value destruction, investors may sell their shares, causing the share price to decline. Therefore, share prices can be a useful indicator of the level of value that financial managers are creating for shareholders. Limitations of Share Prices However, there are drawbacks to using share prices as the sole measure of value creation. Sometimes, even if a financial manager is executing solid financial decisions, share prices may fall due to external factors unrelated to the company’s performance. For example, during a bearish market—where overall market conditions are poor—share prices across the board tend to decline, regardless of individual company performance. On the other hand, a financial manager might be ineffective, but share prices can still rise during a bullish market. In this case, overall positive market conditions or speculative trends may drive up share prices, regardless of the company's internal performance or the quality of its financial management. Therefore, while share prices are a valuable tool for measuring shareholder value creation, they don’t always perfectly reflect the actual performance of a firm or the effectiveness of its financial managers. bank loans A low interest rate on a loan doesn’t define your project’s opportunity cost of capital. Agency Problems and Corporate Governance Corporate Finance 7 A key issue in corporate finance is the potential conflict of interest between managers and shareholders. Managers are entrusted with making decisions that should benefit shareholders, but in some cases, they may act in their own self-interest instead. Conflicts between shareholders’ and managers’ objectives create agency problems. Agency problems arise when agents work for principals. The shareholders are the principals; the managers are their agents. Agency costs are incurred when (1) managers do not attempt to maximise firm value and (2) shareholders incur costs to monitor the managers and constrain their actions. Errors of omission (failing to take good actions, such as launching a new product or closing down an unprofitable division) are often even more serious than errors of commission (undertaking bad actions). Good systems of governance ensure that managers’ hearts are close to shareholders’ pockets. This means well-designed incentives for managers, standards for accounting and disclosure to investors, requirements for boards of directors, and legal sanctions for self-dealing by management. When scandals happen, we say that corporate governance has broken down. When corporations compete effectively and ethically to deliver value to shareholders, we conclude that governance is working properly. Corporate Finance’s recurring themes Corporate finance is all about maximising shareholder value. Maximising shareholder value involves considering the long-term consequences of all decisions, including their effects on stakeholders such as customers, employees, and the environment. Maximising the current stock price does not involve focusing on short-term profits, and increasing shareholder value does not involve price gouging customers, overworking employees, or polluting the environment. In a forward-looking market, even the short-term share price takes these long-term effects into account. Moreover, managers have objectives other than shareholder value. Making Investment Decisions with the Net Present Value Rule (Chapter 6) Forecasting a Project’s Cash Flows NPVs can be used to determine whether a business project is worth undertaking. In this context, the concept of NPV is crucial in capital budgeting decisions. Capital budgeting refers to the process of investing money in fixed assets, such as infrastructure and production investments like plants, machinery, equipment, and so on—essentially, all those investments that will be recovered over the long term and form part of the structure of a firm or company. For capital budgeting, you need to forecast future cash flows. In a business project, as with any other kind of investment, you invest money today with the expectation of recovering that money in the future through a stream of cash flows. These future cash flows arise from the investment’s ability to allow the company to produce goods, and the sale of these goods generates revenue, which then helps recover the initial investment. We have seen that an investment in any asset creates wealth if the discounted value of the future cash flows exceeds the up-front cost. Up to this point, however, we have glossed over the problem of what to discount. When you are faced with this problem, you should stick to five general rules: Rule 1: Discount Cash Flows, Not Profits Net present value depends on the expected future cash flow. Cash flow is simply the difference between cash received and cash paid out. So, only cash flow is relevant in business and capital budgeting decisions. Why? The point is, when assessing an investment project, we need to focus on cash, not profit, for two main reasons: Profit is not money. You can’t buy things with profit; you buy things with cash. Profit can be influenced by subjective, discretionary decisions. For example, when calculating depreciation, amortisation, or valuing inventories, the profit figure can be influenced by the perspective or accounting decisions of those responsible for preparing the financial statements. This makes profit less reliable for decision-making purposes. For these reasons, only cash flow is relevant when assessing a business project. Moreover, the cash flow from a business project must be calculated on an incremental (or differential) basis. In other words, you need to ask, What happens if I undertake this project? What is the change in cash flow if the project goes ahead? We are only concerned with the incremental value created by the project, both in terms of cash inflows and cash outflows. To asses the impact of a project, it is necessary to forecast the new inflows and outflows that are directly associated with that project. There are two types of risk—specific and systematic—and diversification can only get rid of one of them. Specific risks are risks that affect only one company, or only companies in a single industry or of a particular type. strikes, poor management, etc An investor can reduce exposure to these risks by diversifying into unrelated companies Systematic risks are risks that are shared by most other businesses. A downturn in the economy or a collapse of the overall stock market. As a result, you can’t diversify these risks away, no matter how many other companies you move into. Total risk ( ) is the sum of specific and systematic risk. Since systematic risk impacts all firms in a market, diversification alone cannot eliminate it. Through diversification—that is, investing in different securities rather than concentrating all your money in a few—you can eliminate firm-specific risk. When you diversify, you tend to offset higher-than-expected returns on some securities with lower-than-expected returns on others. As a result, on average, you will get what you expected in terms of return. However, increasing the number of securities does not mean you should randomly select them. Instead, you must choose wisely, focusing on securities with low correlations. Naturally, if the Net Present Value (NPV) remains positive in both the optimistic and pessimistic cases, the likelihood of the project being accepted increases. There are two main approaches to developing alternative versions of investment project forecasts. One of these is known as sensitivity analysis. Whenever managers are given a cash-flow forecast in capital budgeting, they try to determine what else may happen and the implications of these possible surprise events. This is called sensitivity analysis. Specifically, in order to increase the chances of their proposals being accepted, financial managers must prepare both pessimistic and optimistic versions of the projected outcomes. These scenarios are based on the worst-case and best-case forecasts for each key variable relevant to the project. For example, in the optimistic version, the financial manager would assume the highest possible number of units sold and the highest sales prices, while estimating the lowest possible production costs. The opposite applies in the pessimistic version. Sensitivity analysis boils down to expressing cash flows in terms of key project variables and then calculating the consequences of misestimating those variables. It forces the manager to identify the crucial determinants of the project’s success and indicates where additional information would be most useful or where design changes may be needed. However, there are two main disadvantages to this approach: First, the definitions of "pessimistic" and "optimistic" are subjective. For instance, one financial manager may define an optimistic forecast as a 10% increase in units sold compared to the pessimistic version, while another may consider 10% too conservative and define optimism as a 20% increase. This highlights the issue of subjectivity and the discretionary power of the evaluator. Second, sensitivity analysis does not consider the correlation between key variables. For example, it is unrealistic to assume that revenue can increase while costs remain at their lowest. If production increases, revenue will likely increase, but so will production costs. For example, if inflation pushes prices to the upper end of your range, it is quite probable that costs will also be inflated. And if sales are unexpectedly high, you may need to invest more in working capital Sensitivity analysis treats variables independently, without accounting for the relationships between them—this limits its accuracy and usefulness in real-world investment decisionmaking. Sensitivity analysis is not a substitute for the NPV rule, but if you know the danger points, you may be able to modify the project or resolve some of the uncertainty before your company undertakes the investment. An alternative to sensitivity analysis is scenario analysis In scenario analysis, we consider at least some of the possible relationships between the key variables that influence the outcome of an investment project. Corporate Finance 90 This method allows for more realistic and integrated forecasting. For instance, if you identify the price of oil as a strategic variable—because it affects the energy costs in your production—you may be particularly concerned about potential increases in oil or gas prices. Starting from that key variable, you analyse how changes in the oil price might affect other variables such as production costs, sales prices, and profit margins. Also, in contrast to sensitivity analysis, scenario analysis does not generally focus on the best and worst outcomes. Instead it tries to identify how a project would be affected by possible major, mutually consistent changes in a project’s environment. By doing so, you can develop different comprehensive scenarios—both optimistic and pessimistic— that reflect these interdependencies and allow for the calculation of corresponding NPVs. Not surprisingly, formal scenario analysis tends to be reserved for major projects or for possible strategies for the entire business. Forms of Market Efficiency We’ve already emphasised one important component of the market efficiency definition: An efficient market incorporates all available information. But what about the second component, “available”? What do we mean by available information? There are three ways to define it, which in turn implies three forms of market efficiency. Weak-form efficiency The weak form of market efficiency means that current share prices reflect all past prices and returns. So, today’s price already incorporates all historical price information. This implies that you cannot use past prices to predict future prices and make abnormal profits because any information you could have inferred from past prices is already in the current stock price. So, knowing past stock prices doesn’t help you predict future returns. Suppose you believe a share is currently undervalued because it doesn’t reflect previous price movements. You might expect the price to rise soon and try to invest to earn a profit. But in a weak-form efficient market, this cannot happen, because the current price already reflects all past movements. There are no patterns or predictable corrections you can exploit—the market has already priced them in. Semi-strong-form efficiency The semi-strong form of market efficiency means that share prices reflect all publicly available information. This includes not only past prices and movements, as in the weak form, but also all other public information, such as Statements from the chief executive officer (CEO), Public announcements or earnings reports, News and information from the mass media, Analyst reports, and more. In this form, the market adjusts quickly to new public information, and investors cannot gain an advantage by trading on information that is publicly available. Any new public information is already reflected in the share price almost immediately. Strong-form efficiency This implies that no investor, not even company insiders such as the chief executive officer (CEO), can consistently earn excess returns because all information—regardless of how it is obtained—is already incorporated into the share price. As a result, no one has a true informational advantage, and the best strategy for investors in such a market is to diversify. Diversification reduces risk and allows investors to earn the market average return, which is all that can be expected in a perfectly efficient market. In contrast, if markets were not strongly efficient, insiders with access to private information—such as CEOs—could exploit it to buy undervalued shares and earn excess profits, which violates the (1 + r) t Corporate Finance 96 principle of strong-form efficiency. Whether a piece of information counts as public or private is sometimes a grey area, so the line between strong-form and semi-strong-form efficiency is blurred. What is the evidence on market efficiency? On one side, some professionals argue that the strong form of market efficiency often holds. They point to empirical studies showing that, on average, institutional investors such as mutual funds, banks, and insurance companies do not consistently achieve returns higher than the overall market. This suggests that even the most informed and educated investors cannot outperform the market in the long run, which supports the idea that prices already reflect all available information. On the other hand, some critics believe that markets tend to be inefficient. Why? Information asymmetry: Even if retail investors put effort into gathering and analysing market data, they cannot access the same level of information as insiders—such as members of the board of directors . Therefore, information is not equally distributed among all market participants. Differences in analytical ability: Not all investors have the same skills or tools to process and interpret information correctly. As a result, mistakes are made, leading to mispricing. Behavioural factors and herd behaviour: At times, investors do not act rationally. Instead, they follow the crowd, leading to herd effects. This can drive prices away from their fundamental values, causing bubbles or crashes. Another important point is that, from a psychological perspective, people tend to be more concerned about losses than motivated by potential gains. In other words, investors are often satisfied with a small profit and prefer to sell shares earlier than they should, simply to avoid the risk of losing. As a result, they may sell too soon, preventing share prices from fully rising to reflect all the available information. This behaviour contributes to market inefficiency, as it causes prices to fall prematurely, rather than adjusting naturally to the true value implied by the information. behavioural finance Behavioural finance is a relatively new field in corporate finance, and it stands in contrast to the efficient market hypothesis. Researchers have attempted to verify the presence of the three forms of market efficiency—weak, semistrong, and strong—in order to support their respective positions. To test weak-form market efficiency, researchers used econometric models to determine whether past share prices were related to current prices. Their findings revealed no such relationship or correlation. In other words, share prices follow a random walk therefore past prices cannot predict future prices. To examine semi-strong form market efficiency, researchers assessed the speed at which share prices adjust to new public information—whether positive or negative. They observed that price changes tend to occur very quickly, indicating that markets react almost immediately to new information. This provided evidence in support of the semi-strong form of efficiency. As for the strong-form market efficiency, researchers found that, on average, even highly informed and professional investors—such as mutual funds, insurance companies, and banks—do not consistently outperform the market in the long run. This suggests that no single investor has privileged access to information, and thus supports the notion that prices reflect all available information, both public and private. This is the process by which firms collect funds directly from the market. secondary transactions. On the other hand, secondary transactions involve the trading of existing securities. In this case, no new capital is raised by the firm. Instead, the ownership of the securities changes hands between investors—one investor sells, and another buys. There is an important relationship between primary issues and secondary transactions. The efficiency of the secondary market enhances the attractiveness and effectiveness of the primary market. If you are a potential investor in newly issued securities, you are more likely to invest if you know that you can later sell those securities quickly, at a fair price, and with ease. In other words, a well-functioning and liquid secondary market increases investor confidence, which in turn facilitates the success of new issues in the primary market. Financial intermediaries Financial intermediaries are institutions such as mutual funds, pension funds, banks, and insurance companies. Their main function is to collect money from investors—primarily households and individuals—and then allocate that capital to those who need it, such as firms, other households, or even banks. Let’s begin with one specific category: mutual funds. Mutual funds are investment vehicles managed by financial institutions. These institutions collect money from investors and pool it into a common fund, which is then invested in a diversified portfolio of assets. Corporate Finance 101 Investors who contribute to the mutual fund receive a proportional (global) share of the overall assets held by the fund A key feature of mutual funds is that they are typically open-ended investments. This means that investors can redeem their shares in the fund at any time, either by selling them on the market or by requesting payment directly from the fund management company. Similarly, investors can increase their stake in the fund by contributing more capital. There is a wide variety of mutual funds, tailored to different investment strategies and preferences. For example, some funds invest exclusively in European, Asian, or U.S. securities. Others specialise in specific sectors, such as utilities, financial firms, technology, or internet companies. Moreover, mutual funds can differ based on the types of instruments they invest in. Some focus solely on shares (equity funds), others on bonds (fixed income funds), while some are balanced funds, which invest in a mix of both equities and bonds. What is the usefulness of investing in mutual funds? Mutual funds allow investors to achieve broad diversification even with relatively small amounts of money. For example, instead of investing a large sum across 20 different individual securities, you can invest as little as €1,000 (or even less) in a mutual fund and gain exposure to a diversified portfolio. This reduces individual asset risk without requiring substantial wealth. Mutual funds are managed by experienced professionals who possess strong financial skills and extensive knowledge of the markets. These fund managers make informed investment decisions on behalf of investors, based on rigorous analysis and years of expertise. If you invest in an open-end mutual fund (which is the most common type), you can easily redeem your investment. There is no need to sell your shares on a secondary market. You simply request repayment directly from the fund management company, which will repurchase your shares at the current net asset value (NAV). This makes it straightforward to access your capital when needed. A pension fund operates differently from mutual funds. It is a fund in which money is collected from both employees and employers during the employee’s working life. The purpose of the fund is to provide financial support to the worker after retirement. At the end of their working life, the worker can either receive a lump-sum payment (liquidation of the investment) or a series of regular payments (a pension) drawn from their accumulated fund. Pension funds are not risk-free investments. The fund managers invest the collected money in financial markets, and therefore, the value of the pension depends on the performance of those investments. The level of risk depends on the type of assets in which the fund is invested. As an investor, you often have the option to choose from a range of pension funds, each with different risk-return profiles—ranging from conservative (e.g., mostly bonds) to aggressive (e.g., mostly equities). Banks collect money mainly from depositors—individuals who place their savings in bank accounts. Using these funds, banks are able to provide financing to households, businesses, and even governments through loans and credit services. In the meantime, the money collected from policyholders is invested in financial and capital markets to generate returns, helping the insurance company remain solvent and profitable. A firm's financing policy often changes over time, primarily depending on the stage of its financial lifecycle. In fact, a firm resembles a human being in that it goes through different stages of development, and its financing needs evolve accordingly. When starting a new business, the entrepreneur typically uses personal savings and funds from family and friends. If the business idea is promising, the firm may be able to raise capital from banks, provided that it appears reliable and capable of repaying the debt. In some cases, angel investors may step in. These are usually wealthy individuals who are willing to finance start-ups, often not purely for financial return but also out of a sense of moral or philosophical commitment to supporting new ventures. Of course, if the capital is repaid, that's preferable, but repayment may not be the primary motivation. Some firms are able to grow successfully without relying on other sources of capital. However, sometimes, companies require the support of a venture capitalist A venture capitalist is a specialised firm that collects capital primarily from institutional investors and other financial institutions. They use this capital to finance the development of new companies—hence the name venture capital, meaning capital for ventures or start-ups. Venture capitalists do not simply provide financial resources; they also offer managerial support, including expertise in production processes, marketing, and distribution services. Of course, the ultimate goal of a venture capitalist is to exit the investment successfully, ideally by making a profit from the equity stake they have taken in the company. The majority of investments fail. This means that venture capitalists must be extremely selective and skilful. They rely on a small portion of highly successful investments—around 20%—to compensate for the losses incurred from the failed ones and to generate a net positive return overall. The initial public offering of stock or IPO In this case, the company supported by venture capitalists may eventually find its way to a financial market, becoming listed or quoted. Through the process of listing, the venture capitalist can liquidate their Corporate Finance 105 initial investment, typically by selling shares in the public market. This is a very complex process, which is why an Initial Public Offering (IPO) is almost never carried out independently by the firm seeking to go public. It requires the expertise and support of an investment bank, which also provides advisory services. The investment bank plays a crucial role in arranging and managing the IPO to ensure its success. A key concern for any company preparing for an IPO is that it must be successful. If the IPO fails, it could severely damage the firm’s reputation. A failed IPO means there is no second chance to go public for a long time, and the company may even lose the opportunity to be quoted on the market altogether. Investment banks often create a pool of other financial institutions, known as underwriters, to help distribute the new shares. These banks take on the risk of underwriting a certain percentage of the shares. If the new shares do not sell out in the market (i.e., they are not fully placed with investors), these underwriters are obliged to buy the unsold shares themselves. This makes underwriting a risky activity for the investment bank. How does the investment bank set the new issue price? One approach is to compare the company with similar publicly listed companies. However, the success of the IPO largely depends on the number of investors willing to buy the new shares. For this reason, the investment bank usually interviews potential large investors—those likely to purchase substantial quantities of shares—to assess demand and price expectations. When setting the issue price, a common phenomenon arises: under-pricing. This refers to the tendency for newly issued shares—whether it's the first issue, second, third, or subsequent ones—to be offered at a price lower than what they might achieve in the open market. One reason for under-pricing is related to the investment bank’s role. Since the investment bank often agrees to underwrite any unsold shares, it reduces its risk by lowering the offering price. A lower price increases the likelihood that all shares will be sold. This strategy also aligns with the interests of the issuing firm. It is in the firm’s best interest to ensure the IPO is successful and fully subscribed. Therefore, by offering shares at a lower price, they increase the chances of attracting investors and successfully placing all shares. In cases where financial markets are inefficient or not well-functioning, the information reflected in share prices may not accurately represent the issuing firm’s true value. This can lead to asymmetric information—a situation where the firm knows more than potential investors. As a result, cautious investors may only be willing to buy shares at a lower price to compensate for the risk of not having full or accurate information. There are costs associated with an Initial Public Offering (IPO), which partly arise from administrative expenses and from fees paid to remunerate the consultancy services of the investment bank. Additionally, there is the cost of under-pricing. All these contribute to the overall cost of an IPO. Once the IPO has been successfully completed, new issues of shares can be sold to the general public in the domestic market. This method is referred to as a general cash offer, meaning that anyone—whether an existing shareholder or a new investor—has the right to purchase the newly issued shares. Alternatively, rights issues refer to the issuance of new shares where existing shareholders are given priority. They are granted the privilege to subscribe to the new shares before they are offered to new investors. Agency problems and corporate governance Agency problems between managers and shareholders. The goal of corporate finance should be the maximisation of shareholders’ value or wealth, and this objective should be shared by everyone in the firm—from the CEO to workers on the assembly line. The relationship between managers and shareholders is known as an agency relationship, where managers act as agents for the shareholders, who are the principals. Managers are expected to act in the best interest of shareholders by making decisions that increase shareholder value. When managers fail to do so and instead act in their own interest, this creates what is known as an agency problem or agency conflict. When managers behave in ways that do not align with the maximisation of shareholder value, it means they may be extracting value from shareholders for their own personal benefit. For instance, a manager might reduce the effort devoted to the firm. perhaps spending work hours playing video games or planning their summer holidays instead of focusing on company tasks. While it is relatively easy to verify whether a manager is physically present at the workplace, it is much harder to measure the intensity and quality of their effort. For example, a manager might frequently go to the coffee machine, but how can we be sure that the remaining time is spent on value-creating activities like net present value (NPV) analysis? Another issue involves perks or private benefits. These are non-wage benefits typically enjoyed by managers, such as a company car, private helicopter, or even access to a personal aircraft. While such perks can be justified when they support business efficiency—for example, enabling fast travel to important meetings—they are sometimes used in a selfish or opportunistic way, bringing personal gain rather than contributing to shareholder value. Empire building, also known as overinvestment In large firms, the managerial structure tends to expand with multiple levels—middle managers, toplevel managers, a Chief Financial Officer (CFO), and possibly even a Chief Executive Officer (CEO). Naturally, in very small firms, all of these roles may be performed by a single person. In such cases, there is little opportunity for individual managers to improve their financial or reputational standing through promotions or role differentiation. As a result, an opportunistic manager might attempt to increase the size of the company not necessarily to maximise shareholder value, but rather to create more managerial positions. This expansion increases their chances for career progression and higher salaries, even if the growth is not justified by sound economic logic. An entrenching investment is a type of investment in a sector or business that a manager knows particularly well. This provides two main advantages for the manager: Reduced risk of failure, as the manager is highly familiar with the business environment and dynamics. Limited external scrutiny, since the manager's specialised knowledge may prevent others— such as shareholders or board members—from properly evaluating the investment decisions. Corporate Finance 107 However, from the shareholders' perspective, entrenching investments can be problematic. While they may reduce risk, they also reduce potential returns. Remember the fundamental principle: higher risk is usually associated with higher potential return. This does not mean that all investments should be high-risk, but consistently choosing only lowrisk, entrenching investments will eventually destroy shareholder value, as the manager will likely pursue projects with low or even negative Net Present Value (NPV). Risk taking or the «quiet life» approach and short-termism Why should I, as a manager, take risks if I can live a quiet life? I might prefer to invest only in more or less riskless projects. This way, I avoid taking any personal risks and reduce the chance of being fired. But who cares? You, the shareholder, care. This is because, once again, the financial manager is not fulfilling their duties properly. By only investing in low-risk projects, the manager is avoiding responsibility rather than acting in the best interests of the shareholders. This leads to value destruction over time. Another related concept is short-termism. This occurs when a manager focuses on investments that offer quick returns—projects with a very short recovery period—because this reduces the perceived risk of failure in the short term. Managers are compensated based on financial indicators, such as Return on Capital or Economic Value Added. While these indicators can be useful and objective to some extent, they are also partially under managerial control. For instance, a manager can improve these metrics not by enhancing the real performance of the firm, but simply by cutting costs. To increase Return on Equity, a manager might decide to reduce research and development expenses or cut back on employee training programmes. Although this leads to short-term improvements in financial indicators, it harms the company’s long-term value. Without sustained investment in R&D, the firm’s competitive position deteriorates over time. Likewise, if employee skills are not regularly updated, the firm may lose market share as competitors with better-trained staff become more productive and innovative. How is it possible to reduce agency conflicts between managers and shareholders? Typically, a "stick and carrot" approach is adopted. The "stick" refers to control mechanisms, primarily through monitoring the business. In small or medium enterprises, shareholder control over managers is relatively straightforward. Now, who is responsible for monitoring? Primarily, it is the shareholders. However, we must distinguish between small or medium-sized companies and large multinational corporations. There is often a close relationship between shareholders and managers. In many cases, shareholders are blockholders—that is, they own a significant portion of the company’s shares. The proximity between shareholders and managers facilitates effective oversight. Shareholders can directly question managers and hold them accountable In contrast, in large multinational companies, ownership is typically dispersed among many shareholders, each holding only a small percentage of shares. These shareholders often reside in different countries from where the company or its headquarters is located, making direct monitoring impractical. In such cases, the responsibility for oversight is delegated to the board of directors. The board of directors is composed of individuals who are nominated and dismissed by the shareholders. One might think, “Well, that’s fine—once the board of directors is appointed, shareholders are safe, and shareholder value will be maximised.” Not quite. That is not always the case, as there are several issues that can compromise the effectiveness of the board of directors. One key issue is independence. Corporate Finance 108 It is quite common for some board members to also be company managers—for example, the Chief Executive Officer or the Chief Financial Officer. This can be both appropriate and beneficial, as these individuals are responsible for the company’s main strategic decisions. However, having managers on the board is not inherently good or bad. Independence, in this context, refers to achieving a proper balance between executive directors (who are also managers) and non-executive directors (who are not involved in dayto-day management). A well-functioning board requires this balance. Of course, a manager cannot be considered an independent director Managers are appointed—and, when necessary, dismissed—by the board of directors, which makes their position inherently dependent. An independent director, on the other hand, is someone who has no personal or business relationship with the company, is not related to any of the managers, and is not a manager themselves. board size The board of directors is responsible for a wide range of tasks, as it must determine the overall strategy of the firm in areas such as production, distribution, organisational structure, and human resource management. Given the breadth of these responsibilities, it might seem that the larger the board, the better, since more members can be assigned to oversee specific functions and dedicate adequate time to each. However, a board that is too small—comprising only two or three individuals—may be overburdened, with a limited capacity to manage all these complex tasks effectively over-boarding Overboarding refers to situations where the same individuals serve on multiple boards— sometimes two, three, or even more. When directors sit on too many boards, the amount of time and attention they can devote to each firm is naturally limited. As a result, their effectiveness in overseeing company affairs may decline. To address this issue, some legal systems impose limits on the number of directorships an individual can hold simultaneously, in order to ensure that board members are able to perform their duties properly. election The election of board members is sometimes held annually, meaning there is a yearly renewal of votes. However, in some cases, only part of the board is re-elected every three years. Over time, board members who stay for longer periods can develop closer relationships with the managers. They may form friendships or even become colleagues, potentially compromising their objectivity and ability to effectively monitor the company's activities. Therefore, it is generally a good idea to hold elections for board members at least once a year to maintain fresh perspectives and ensure accountability. Auditors Auditors are external organisations or firms that review a company's accounting records as well as its overall economic and financial situation. Rival Companies Rival companies can act as monitoring agents, especially in the case of listed firms. When a company is listed on the stock exchange, it becomes susceptible to takeovers, including hostile takeovers—those not supported or initiated by the current shareholders or management. In such cases, the ownership of the firm can shift from the existing shareholders to new ones. It is relatively easy to acquire shares of a listed company, especially when the stock price is low, which often indicates poor performance. This creates an opportunity for other companies or investors to buy up shares and attempt a takeover. Therefore, the threat of hostile takeovers serves as a disciplinary mechanism for managers. To avoid losing control of the firm, it is in the managers' interest to perform well, maintain strong financial performance, and ensure high share prices, thereby making the company a less attractive target for hostile acquisition. The "carrot" refers to incentives, which aim to encourage managers to act in alignment with shareholders' interests. How can we incentivise managers to act in the best interests of shareholders? One common method is through compensation schemes. In fact, a large portion of managerial compensation is not fixed; rather, it is variable and performance-based. This variable component is often linked to financial indicators such as Return on Capital (ROC), Return on Equity (ROE), Return on Assets (ROA), or Economic Value Added (EVA). The advantage of using such ratios is that they are relatively easy to calculate, relying solely on accounting data taken from financial statements. However, the drawback is that the calculation and presentation of these figures are influenced by the managers themselves. For example, managers might inflate inventory levels, reduce amortisation, or cut training and research & development expenses in order to artificially boost short-term results. Another popular form of incentive is stock-based compensation, where a manager’s pay is tied to the company's share price performance—either through stock options or by awarding company shares directly. This aligns managerial interests with those of shareholders, as both parties benefit from rising stock prices. However, this method also has a downside: share prices are affected by broader market trends, not just the firm’s internal performance In a bull market, stock prices may rise regardless of managerial ability. Conversely, in a bear market, prices may fall even when a manager is highly competent and the firm is well-managed. Thus, a manager might be unfairly penalised or rewarded due to general market movements rather than their own performance. Stakeholder capitalism and responsible business The goal of a firm, traditionally, is the maximisation of shareholder value. This is considered the classical capitalist objective of a firm: to act in the interests of its owners by increasing their wealth. Corporate Finance 110 According to this view, maximising shareholder value also indirectly serves the interests of other stakeholders—such as suppliers, customers, employees, managers, and even the government—by ensuring the firm remains profitable, competitive, and sustainable. This traditional approach was famously advocated by Milton Friedman, who wrote a widely cited article in 1970. In it, he stated that "the business of business is business", meaning that a company’s sole responsibility is to maximise profits, provided it respects the law and the rules of fair competition. Under this view, firms are not expected to pursue social or environmental goals unless doing so directly contributes to profit generation. One of the strengths of the shareholder value approach is its clarity. It offers a clear decision-making rule that can guide everyone within the organisation—from the CEO to workers. If the firm is listed on the financial market, this goal becomes even more transparent: maximising shareholder value simply means ensuring that share prices rise as much as possible. However, Milton Friedman argued that shareholder value maximisation—the foundation of shareholder capitalism—is appropriate only under three key conditions that support this approach. The first condition is that government policies must ensure that companies behave in a socially responsible manner. In his 1970 article, Friedman explained that democratically elected governments are responsible for implementing the electoral programmes for which they have received a mandate from the voters. These programmes are then translated into laws and regulations, which companies are expected to follow. As long as firms comply with these rules, they are considered to be acting appropriately. Friedman argued that it is not the role of companies to decide what is socially or ethically right; instead, this is the role of the government, acting on behalf of the public. A company’s duty is to maximise profits, provided it operates within the legal and regulatory framework defined by the state. He illustrates this with the example of smoking In most countries, smoking is not completely prohibited, despite its health risks. Rather, governments typically impose taxes on tobacco products as a means of discouraging consumption. This reflects a democratic compromise: rather than banning smoking outright—which would dissatisfy both smokers and cigarette producers—governments use fiscal tools to reduce consumption. According to Friedman, this approach demonstrates how governments can balance public health concerns with personal and economic freedoms. Therefore, Friedman concludes that as long as a company respects the legal rules established by the government, it is not required to take on broader social responsibilities Maximising shareholder value allows investors to pursue social objectives. This means that companies should not engage in charitable activities According to Friedman, the role of a firm is not to decide how profits should be used for social causes. Instead, its responsibility is to maximise shareholder wealth, thereby giving individual shareholders the freedom to support the causes they personally value. For example, suppose a company is considering whether to donate to a cancer research organisation or to an environmental initiative fighting climate change. These are both worthy causes, but there is no reason the firm itself should decide between them. Instead, by distributing profits to shareholders, each individual investor can independently choose which cause to support—whether it’s cancer research, environmental protection, or something else entirely. In short, maximising shareholder value enhances personal freedom, allowing shareholders to allocate their wealth according to their own ethical and social preferences. This avoids the problem of a company making moral or political decisions on behalf of its investors and ensures that business remains focused on its primary economic function Corporate Finance 111 Maximising shareholder value requires companies to invest in stakeholders. This means that by focusing on shareholder value maximisation, a firm also indirectly creates value for other stakeholders. Shareholders hold a residual claim on the firm’s cash flows—meaning they receive what is left after all other obligations to stakeholders (such as employees, suppliers, and creditors) have been met. Therefore, the greater the total cash flow generated by the firm, the greater the potential value available not only to shareholders but also to stakeholder In order to maximise shareholder value, companies must ensure that stakeholders remain engaged and satisfied. If customers are dissatisfied, they will stop buying the company’s products. If employees are poorly treated, they may leave or underperform. If banks and investors do not trust the firm, they will stop financing it. Thus, a firm cannot achieve sustainable shareholder value without also investing in relationships with stakeholders. There are several criticisms of the three conditions outlined in support of shareholder value maximisation. First, even in democratically elected governments, problems can arise. Lobbying—the influence exerted by powerful groups or individuals—can distort the policymaking process. In many cases, the electoral programmes through which governments are elected are heavily influenced by lobbying groups, and thus may reflect special interests rather than the general will of the electorate. Second, politicians are often primarily concerned with re-election. The most significant fear for many members of parliament is not being re-elected. As a result, when their mandate is nearing its end, politicians may be reluctant to introduce unpopular legislation, even if it is necessary. For instance, they may postpone implementing new taxes to avoid upsetting voters close to an election. Typically, new taxation measures are introduced at the beginning of a government’s term, when political capital is strongest. As the end of the mandate approaches, the likelihood of passing unpopular measures decreases, as politicians prioritise electoral approval over long-term policy objectives. Thirdly, when it comes to particularly sensitive and complex issues—such as immigration in Europe or decisions about end-of-life care for terminally ill individuals—it is difficult to translate general ethical or moral intentions into concrete legislation These issues are deeply divisive and often resist simple regulatory solutions. For all these reasons, even democratically elected governments may not be the most effective or reliable mechanism for ensuring responsible corporate behaviour under a shareholder value maximisation model. Another criticism relates to the lack of comparative advantage for companies in relying on shareholders to serve broader social interests. According to the shareholder value approach, firms should avoid engaging in charitable or socially responsible activities and should instead maximise profits, leaving it to individual shareholders to decide how to allocate their wealth. However, this can be impractical. For example, suppose a group of shareholders wants the company to replace plastic packaging with a more sustainable alternative, such as cardboard. In theory, these shareholders could fund a lobbying group to pressure the government into banning plastic packaging. But this route is expensive, time-consuming, and uncertain in terms of outcome. On the other hand, the company itself could make the switch directly and efficiently, without needing new legislation. Now, what must the company do before deciding whether to build the day-care facility? Capital budgeting, it needs to conduct an assessment. That means evaluating the cash flows, both inflows and outflows, associated with the project. The outflows are relatively easy to estimate—they include the initial investment, the cost of managing the day care, staff salaries, equipment, food, toys, etc. However, estimating the inflows is much more difficult You might say that inflows are represented by the opportunity cost savings —i.e., not having to pay for private childcare elsewhere. This is known as stakeholder capitalism, or stakeholder value maximisation. However, this approach is more of a philosophy than a practical framework. Why? Because it raises a fundamental question: How can a firm possibly address the interests of all stakeholders simultaneously? In reality, these interests often conflict with one another. For example, what benefits employees (e.g., higher wages, better benefits) might reduce profits for shareholders. What pleases customers (e.g., lower prices) might limit resources for innovation or sustainability. These tensions make it extremely difficult to identify a clear, consistent objective. If you ask a manager to define a goal that satisfies all stakeholders, after a few days of reflection they might feel like they’re going mad—because in practice, it’s nearly impossible to implement such a vague and conflicting set of targets. Other firms, lacking this advantage, found it much harder—or even impossible—to adapt. Many of them were forced to shut down, unless they received financial support from their governments. Call Provision A call provision is a right held by the issuing firm that allows it to repay its bonds earlier than the agreed maturity date. For example, suppose a bond contract has a maturity of 10 years. With a call provision, the company has the option to repay the bonds before the 10-year term ends. Why would a firm choose to repay its bonds early? One common reason is changes in market interest rates. Suppose a company issues bonds at a fixed interest rate during a period when interest rates are relatively high. If, over time, general interest rates decline, the firm might find it financially advantageous to repay its existing bonds early and refinance by issuing new bonds at a lower interest rate. This strategy reduces the overall cost of borrowing. In short, a call provision enables the issuing firm to reduce interest expenses over time by taking advantage of favourable changes in market interest rates. Sinking Fund A sinking fund is a method of repaying bonds that serves as an alternative to a call provision. In this case, it is up to the issuing firm to decide whether to repay bondholders by either: repaying the face (nominal) value of the bonds, or repurchasing the bonds on the open market. The decision between these two options is primarily financial. If the market value of the bonds is higher than the nominal value, the firm will choose to repay the nominal value. Conversely, if the market value is lower than the nominal value, the firm will repurchase the bonds on the market at the lower price.
