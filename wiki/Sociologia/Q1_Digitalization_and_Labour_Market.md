# Q1: The Effects of Digitalization on Labour Market Dynamics

## 1. The Two Main Analytical Perspectives

### A) [[SBTC]] — Skill-Biased Technological Change (Traditional View)
Technology privileges **highly educated workers** while replacing **low-skilled** workers with machines.
- **Logic:** Machines substitute manual/repetitive work → low-skilled workers lose out.
- **Result:** Rising inequality between high-skilled and low-skilled workers.
- **Limitation:** Does not explain why educated white-collar workers are also losing jobs.

### B) [[RBTC]] — Routine-Biased Technological Change (More Recent & Disruptive)
Technology targets **routine tasks**, not just low skills — regardless of education level.
- **Routine tasks threatened:** Accounting, legal administration, clerical work → even white-collar jobs.
- **Non-routine tasks resilient:** Complex analytical tasks (high-end) and interactive/manual services (low-end).
- **Key consequence:** [[Labour Market Polarization]] — "Hollowing out of the Middle".

---

## 2. [[Labour Market Polarization]] — "The Hollowing Out"

| Segment | Type of Work | Effect of Digitalization |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-End** | Management, research, creative analysis | ✅ Resilient — complements ICT |
| **Middle-Class** | Clerical work, accounting, routine cognitive | ❌ Under threat — highly automatable |
| **Low-End** | Cleaning, waitressing, personal services | ✅ Resilient — difficult to automate |

> **Key Research Finding:** Frey & Osborne (2017) estimated **47% of US jobs** at high risk. The OECD (2018): **14% very high risk**, **32% high risk**.
> Note: *Occupation-based* approaches (full jobs) overestimate risk vs *task-based* approaches (~9%).

## 3. [[Platform Work]] — Chapter 13 (Picot)

### Is Platform Work Inevitable?
- **Picot's argument:** Platform work is a **"social product"** — shaped by social, economic, institutional and political conditions. Countries *can* limit their growth.

### Two Types:
1. **Cloud Work:** Delivered online (freelance design, coding).
2. **Gig Work:** Delivered on-site (food delivery, ride-sharing).

### The "Segregated Model":
- **Spain/Italy (14%):** Platform work is a **necessity** → high unemployment, weak protections.
- **Finland (4%):** Platform work is a **choice** → strong social safety nets.

### Political Limits:
- **Trade Associations:** Established firms fight platforms as unfair competition (e.g., taxis vs Uber).
- **Trade Unions:** Struggle to organize self-employed platform workers, but can maintain political pressure.

---

## 4. Political Consequences: The Rise of Populism
- Workers at high automation risk are a key **voting bloc for radical right** parties.
- The shrinking middle class fuels **populism** across OECD countries.
- Generous welfare states can *partially* buffer this, but cannot prevent the political turn.
- See also: [[Q2_Digitalization_and_Welfare_State]] for welfare state responses.

---

## 5. Welfare State Responses
- **Dominant trend:** [[Social Investment State]] — education, vocational training, lifelong learning.
- **Radical proposal:** [[Universal Basic Income]] — gaining public traction but not yet mainstream.
- **Why redistribution preferred by affected workers:** Immediate support > long-term retraining; UBI reduces labor market dependence.

---

## Exam Summary Sentence
*"Digitalization transforms labour markets through SBTC (privileging high-skilled workers) and RBTC (eliminating routine tasks, hollowing out the middle class). This polarization fuels inequality, precarious platform work, and politically, the rise of populism. The welfare state responds primarily through social investment, though UBI is gaining traction as a more radical alternative."*
