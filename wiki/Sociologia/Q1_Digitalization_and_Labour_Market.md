# Q1: The Effects of Digitalization on Labour Market Dynamics

## 1. The Two Main Analytical Perspectives

### A) [[SBTC]] — Skill-Biased Technological Change (Traditional View)
Technology privileges **highly educated workers** while replacing **low-skilled** workers with machines.
- **Logic:** Machines substitute manual/repetitive work → low-skilled workers lose out.
- **Result:** Rising inequality between high-skilled and low-skilled workers.
- **Limitation:** Does not explain why educated white-collar workers are also losing jobs.

### B) [[RBTC]] — Routine-Biased Technological Change (More Recent & Disruptive)
Technology targets **routine tasks**, not just low skills — regardless of education level.
- **Routine tasks threatened:** Accounting, legal administration, clerical work → even white-collar jobs.
- **Non-routine tasks resilient:** Complex analytical tasks (high-end) and interactive/manual services (low-end).
- **Key consequence:** [[Labour Market Polarization]] — "Hollowing out of the Middle".

## 2. [[Labour Market Polarization]] — "The Hollowing Out"

| Segment | Type of Work | Effect of Digitalization |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-End** | Management, research, creative analysis | ✅ Resilient — complements ICT |
| **Middle-Class** | Clerical work, accounting, routine cognitive | ❌ Under threat — highly automatable |
| **Low-End** | Cleaning, waitressing, personal services | ✅ Resilient — difficult to automate |

> **Key Research Finding:** Frey & Osborne (2017) estimated **47% of US jobs** at high risk. The OECD (2018): **14% very high risk**, **32% high risk**.
> Note: *Occupation-based* approaches (full jobs) overestimate risk vs *task-based* approaches (~9%).

### 🌏 Evidenza Empirica: Il Caso della Corea del Sud
La transizione digitale in Corea del Sud offre una validazione diretta del processo di polarizzazione indotto da [[RBTC]]:
- **Boom del Terziario Avanzato:** Registrati **+492.000 occupati** nel settore dei servizi digitali.
- **Contrazione dell'Industria Fisica:** Perdita netta di **-73.000 posti di lavoro** nel manifatturiero e **-125.000** nelle costruzioni per via della massiccia robotizzazione e automazione di fabbrica che sostituisce i ruoli di routine a medio-bassa qualifica.
- **Divario di Genere:** La crisi colpisce prevalentemente comparti fisici tradizionali a prevalenza maschile, mentre l'occupazione femminile cresce trainata dal terziario avanzato.

## 3. [[Platform Work]] — Chapter 13 (Picot)

### Is Platform Work Inevitable?
- **Picot's argument:** Platform work is a **"social product"** — shaped by social, economic, institutional and political conditions. Countries *can* limit their growth.

### Two Types:
1. **Cloud Work:** Delivered online (freelance design, coding).
2. **Gig Work:** Delivered on-site (food delivery, ride-sharing).

### The "Segregated Model":
- **Spain/Italy (14%):** Platform work is a **necessity** → high unemployment, weak protections.
- **Finland (4%):** Platform work is a **choice** → strong social safety nets.

### Political Limits:
- **Trade Associations:** Established firms fight platforms as unfair competition (e.g., taxis vs Uber).
- **Trade Unions:** Struggle to organize self-employed platform workers, but can maintain political pressure.

## 4. Political Consequences: The Rise of Populism
- Workers at high automation risk are a key **voting bloc for radical right** parties.
- The shrinking middle class fuels **populism** across OECD countries.
- Generous welfare states can *partially* buffer this, but cannot prevent the political turn.
- See also: [[Q2_Digitalization_and_Welfare_State]] for welfare state responses.

---

## 5. Welfare State Responses
- **Dominant trend:** [[Social Investment State]] — education, vocational training, lifelong learning.
- **Radical proposal:** [[Universal Basic Income]] — gaining public traction but not yet mainstream.
- **Why redistribution preferred by affected workers:** Immediate support > long-term retraining; UBI reduces labor market dependence.

## 🏆 Full Exam-Ready Essay Answer (C1/C2 Academic English)

When we study how digitalization is changing the labor market, we can see that it is challenging many traditional ideas about work. To understand this structural shift, sociologists usually compare two main theories: Skill-Biased Technological Change (SBTC) and Routine-Biased Technological Change (RBTC). The older SBTC theory, which was very popular in the late 20th century, suggests that technological innovation helps highly educated and skilled workers, while replacing low-skilled workers. According to this view, computers and machines create a wider wage gap and increase inequality between educated professionals and unskilled workers. However, SBTC has a major limit: it cannot explain why many white-collar, college-educated workers are also losing their jobs today. This is why the newer RBTC theory is so important. It argues that automation does not target a worker's degree, but rather the routine nature of their tasks. This means that routine tasks—both manual and cognitive, like bookkeeping, administration, or factory assembly—can easily be automated, regardless of the worker's education. On the other hand, non-routine tasks are much safer. These include high-level jobs that require creativity and strategy, as well as low-level manual services like healthcare assistance or cleaning, which machines cannot easily do.

This replacement of routine work leads directly to labor market polarization, which is often called the "hollowing out" of the middle class. As mid-level jobs disappear, the labor market splits into two: high-paying, secure professional jobs at the top, and low-paying, insecure service jobs at the bottom. We can see a clear example of this process in South Korea's digital transition. Due to rapid factory automation and the use of industrial robots, the country created about 492,000 new digital service jobs for highly skilled urban professionals, but at the same time, it lost 73,000 manufacturing jobs and 125,000 construction jobs. This shows how mid-level routine workers are pushed out of the market. This shift also has a gender aspect: the job losses mainly affected traditional, male-dominated manufacturing sectors, while the new service and welfare jobs absorbed more female workers.

This decline of stable, middle-class jobs has led to the rapid growth of platform work in the gig economy. However, as Georg Picot argues, platform work is not just a natural result of technology; it is a social and political product. Picot uses a "segregated model" to show that different countries have very different levels of platform work based on their laws and welfare systems. In Southern European countries like Italy and Spain, where youth unemployment is high and social security is weak, platform work is an economic necessity for survival, leading to a high platform density of around fourteen percent. But in social-democratic countries like Finland, where the welfare state is strong, gig work is just a voluntary choice for extra, flexible income, resulting in a low density of only four percent. Because they work outside traditional contracts, platform workers lack collective bargaining and social benefits, creating a new insecure group of workers often called the "precariat."

Politically, this hollowing out of the middle class has a big impact on democracy. Workers who lose their jobs or feel insecure are much more likely to vote for far-right and populist parties across OECD countries. In response, modern welfare states have adopted the "Social Investment State" approach. This model focuses on active policies like education, human capital, and lifelong retraining to help workers adapt. However, critics argue that this creates a "skills mismatch" and suffers from the "Matthew Effect," where retraining mostly benefits younger, already privileged people, while older workers struggle with learning barriers. Because of this, many affected workers prefer direct financial help rather than retraining, which has increased support for radical ideas like a Universal Basic Income (UBI) to separate survival from having a job.
