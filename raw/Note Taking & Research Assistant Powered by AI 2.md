1\. Classification by Maturity

**Long-Term Debt** The most common form of long-term financing is the **bond**. These can take several forms:

- **Straight Bonds:** Standard bonds where the firm pays periodic interest and repays the principal at maturity. They may include special features like a *call provision* (the firm's right to repay the bond early if market interest rates drop) or a *sinking fund* (the firm's choice to repay the nominal value or repurchase the bonds on the open market).- **Convertible Bonds:** These include an option that allows the bondholder to convert their debt into company shares, offering the security of debt with the upside potential of equity.- **Bonds with Warrants:** Similar to convertibles, but the right to purchase new shares (the warrant) is a separate instrument that can be traded independently from the bond itself.- **Exotic or Unusual Bonds:** Financial markets have engineered many highly specific bonds, including:
	- *Asset-Backed Securities (ABS):* Bonds secured by a pool of underlying assets (like mortgage loans) transformed into liquid securities via a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV).- *Catastrophe (CAT) Bonds:* Bonds where interest or principal payments are reduced or suspended if a specific natural disaster occurs.- *Contingent Convertibles (COCOs):* High-yield bonds that automatically convert into equity if the firm's capital falls below a certain threshold or during financial distress.- *Longevity and Mortality Bonds:* Used by pension funds and life insurers, where payments change based on whether statistical mortality rates increase or decrease.- *Pay-in-Kind (PIK) Bonds:* Bonds that allow the issuer to pay interest by issuing additional bonds instead of cash.- *Equity-Linked Bonds:* Floating-rate bonds tied to the performance of a stock market index.- *Credit-Linked Bonds:* Interest payments fluctuate based on the firm's credit rating.- *Reverse Floaters:* Speculative bonds where interest payments rise when a reference market rate falls.- *Step-up Bonds:* Bonds where interest payments incrementally increase over time.

**Short-Term Debt** When long-term financing doesn't cover all capital needs, firms rely on short-term debt.

- **Bank Loans:** Usually short-term (though sometimes spanning a few years), these include *revolving lines of credit* that a firm can draw upon up to a limit. For extremely large amounts, banks may pool resources to offer a *syndicated loan*.- **Commercial Paper:** Short-term debt (maturing within a few months, generally up to 270 days) issued directly to investors by the firm, bypassing traditional banks to secure more flexible and cheaper financing.- **Medium-Term Notes (MTNs):** Function similarly to commercial paper but carry a slightly longer maturity, ranging from one to several years.

2\. Other Crucial Distinctions in Debt

To truly understand the debt structure of a company, a top-tier financial manager must also evaluate debt based on the following features:

- **Interest Rate (Fixed vs. Floating):** Fixed debt pays a locked-in interest rate. Floating debt has interest payments that rise or fall based on a variable benchmark rate (like LIBOR or EURIBOR).- **Security (Secured vs. Unsecured):** A secured loan or bond is backed by specific collateral (like property or machinery). If the firm defaults, the lenders have the right to seize and sell that specific asset to recover their money.- **Seniority (Senior vs. Junior/Subordinated):** This dictates the priority of repayment if the company goes bankrupt. Senior debt holders are first in line and must be fully repaid before any junior or subordinated creditors see a single euro.- **Currency:** Debt does not have to be issued in the firm's home country. A European firm, for example, can issue debt denominated in US dollars or Japanese yen
