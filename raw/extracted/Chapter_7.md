As a result a portfolio standard deviation cannot be zero. Specific and Systematic Risk You can reduce the risk of a portfolio by including securities with low covariances and low correlations. However, it is impossible to completely eliminate risk, because, generally speaking, the returns of securities traded within the same market tend to move together. There are two types of risk—specific and systematic—and diversification can only get rid of one of them. Specific risks are risks that affect only one company, or only companies in a single industry or of a particular type. strikes, poor management, etc An investor can reduce exposure to these risks by diversifying into unrelated companies Systematic risks are risks that are shared by most other businesses. A downturn in the economy or a collapse of the overall stock market. As a result, you can’t diversify these risks away, no matter how many other companies you move into. Total risk ( ) is the sum of specific and systematic risk. Since systematic risk impacts all firms in a market, diversification alone cannot eliminate it. Through diversification—that is, investing in different securities rather than concentrating all your money in a few—you can eliminate firm-specific risk. When you diversify, you tend to offset higher-than-expected returns on some securities with lower-than-expected returns on others. As a result, on average, you will get what you expected in terms of return. However, increasing the number of securities does not mean you should randomly select them. Instead, you must choose wisely, focusing on securities with low correlations. When you have properly diversified your portfolio, you reach a level of risk that corresponds to market risk, which cannot be eliminated through diversification. You have to diversify based on the market where the specific security is traded. To do this effectively, you typically use a proxy for diversification. For example, you can invest in other securities within the same market as the specific stock. Common proxies include indices like the S&P 500, the Dow Jones Industrial Average, or any other index you prefer. To answer this, we must rely on Markowitz’s theory of efficient portfolios and the concept of the efficient frontier. Markowitz developed a method for creating an optimal portfolio for each investor, starting from the set of securities traded in a financial market. He argued that, as investors, we should consider all the available securities in the market. if you refer to the graph, you will notice several dots. The opportunity region represents all the possible combinations of the securities traded in the market. In other words, you are selecting from all available securities, and what differentiates one combination from another is simply the percentage of the total investment allocated to each security. As you can imagine, since we are considering all securities in the market, these combinations— also referred to as market portfolios—are diversified portfolios.
