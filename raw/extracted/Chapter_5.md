The Payback Rule A project’s payback period is found by counting the number of years it takes before the cumulative cash flow equals the initial investment. It answers the following question: How long will it take to recover the initial investment? The payback rule states that a project should be accepted if its payback period is less than some specified cut-off period. The shorter the forecasted recovery period, the better. Why? Because money never sleeps. The quicker you can recover your investment, the faster you can reinvest that money into new projects, generating more value. an advantage is that it’s simple. Payback will tend to favour many poor short-lived projects and reject many good long-lived ones. The payback period does not take into account the opportunity cost of capital, which is the minimum level of return demanded by investors, including the risk associated with the project. If you don’t consider this, all the cash flows—regardless of when they are forecasted—are weighted equally. From a scientific point of view, this is incorrect, as future cash flows should be discounted to reflect their value over time. The choice of a cut-off period is arbitrary. There is no clear criterion for choosing this cut-off period. Accounting Rate of Return Shareholders often calculate profitability measures, such as the company’s profits divided by its assets. Only cash flows. Can you see cash flows in this approach? No. It uses profits, which are not as objective as cash flows. Accounting measures of profitability provide an unreliable criterion for the firm’s investment decisions for at least two reasons: Accounting return ignores the time value of money. The accounting rate of return depends on accounting profits, which are different from cash flow. Moreover, accounting return depends on the average profits over the lifetime of the project, and doesn’t take into account when these profits are received. No clear benchmark. There is no natural benchmark for judging whether a project’s accounting return is attractive. Managers sometimes compare the profitability of a proposed project with the profitability of the company as a whole. But the success of the firm’s past investments should not affect whether to go ahead with the current proposal. What matters are the cash flows that the proposed project is likely to generate and the opportunity cost of the capital that is tied up in the project. The IRR Rule The internal rate of return rule states that the firm should accept an investment project if the opportunity cost of capital is less than the internal rate of return. You can see that in the aforementioned figure. If the opportunity cost of capital is less than the IRR, then the project has a positive NPV when discounted at the opportunity cost of capital. If it is greater than the IRR, the project has a negative NPV because shareholders could earn a higher return by investing their funds in the capital market. When the discount rate equals the IRR, the NPV is zero. At this point, you can choose to go ahead with the project or not, as the return is just equal to the minimum required return (the opportunity cost of capital). The internal rate of return on our project tells us how high the opportunity cost of capital would need to be before the project should be rejected That can be good to know, for, although we might not be able to put a precise number on the project’s cost of capital, we might nevertheless be confident that it was less than . You can understand, therefore, why a manager may find it helpful to know the project’s IRR. The rule works as the net present value rule whenever the NPV of a project is a continuously declining function of the discount rate. Although, properly stated, the two criteria are formally equivalent, the internal rate of return rule contains several pitfalls. Pitfall 1—Lending or Borrowing? Not all cash-flow streams have NPVs that decline as the discount rate increases. When it comes to investments, there’s no problem using either the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) or the Net Present Value (NPV) approach. However, when it comes to borrowing, things change because the intended return approach is different—it’s the opposite. Pitfall 2—Multiple Rates of Return Multiple rates of return occur whenever an investment project has changes in sign. For example, you may first have an outflow, then an inflow, then another outflow, and so on. Each time there is a change in sign, you get an internal rate of return (IRR). The number of IRRs corresponds to the number of sign changes. This situation can create a puzzle: which IRR should we choose? There is no correct answer in such cases, which is why it's best to discard the IRR approach when multiple sign changes occur and instead rely on the NPV method. Choosing Capital Investments When Resources Are Limited Suppose, however, that there are limitations on the amount of capital available to the firm. In this case, you need to select the package of projects that is within the company’s resources yet gives the highest possible net present value. To do so, you should pick the projects that offer the highest net present value per euro of initial outlay. This ratio is known as the profitability index. A positive PI (greater than ) means that the present value of cash inflows exceeds the initial investment, suggesting that the project will generate more value than it costs.
